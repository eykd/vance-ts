# Cognitive Biases in Research

Eight biases that systematically distort information gathering and interpretation, with countermeasures.

## 1. Confirmation Bias
**How it manifests:** Phrasing searches to confirm existing beliefs ("benefits of X" instead of "effects of X"), clicking only aligned results, selectively highlighting supportive findings.
**Countermeasure:** Search for disconfirming evidence first. Use neutral search terms. Write down your hypothesis before searching so you can honestly evaluate whether evidence supports it.

## 2. Anchoring Bias
**How it manifests:** First information encountered (Google featured snippet, top search result) disproportionately frames all subsequent understanding. Physicians were 10% more likely to list a diagnosis when primed by another doctor's suggestion.
**Countermeasure:** Consult multiple sources before forming any initial impression. Start with review articles or meta-analyses that synthesize multiple perspectives.

## 3. Availability Heuristic
**How it manifests:** Judging importance by how easily examples come to mind. Top-ranked search results feel more "true" because they're more visible. Frequently repeated claims feel credible even when originating from a single unreliable source.
**Countermeasure:** Look beyond the first page of results. Seek statistical base rates rather than anecdotes. Check whether a frequently seen claim traces back to a single original source.

## 4. Authority Bias
**How it manifests:** Accepting claims from prestigious sources without scrutinizing evidence. Credentials and affiliations serve as shortcuts that reduce careful processing.
**Countermeasure:** Evaluate evidence independently of the source's prestige. Verify authorities are speaking within their actual area of expertise.

## 5. Dunning-Kruger Effect
**How it manifests:** Novice researchers overestimate their evaluation ability after reading a few articles. Feeling like an expert leads to premature research termination.
**Countermeasure:** Use structured evaluation frameworks (SIFT). Acknowledge the limits of surface-level familiarity.

## 6. Bandwagon Effect
**How it manifests:** Treating widely shared content as true because "everyone is sharing it." Qualitative bandwagon signals (agreement comments) influence credibility judgments even more than share counts.
**Countermeasure:** Distinguish between expert consensus (valuable signal) and popular virality (potentially meaningless).

## 7. Source Amnesia
**How it manifests:** Remembering information but forgetting its origin — unable to distinguish whether a statistic came from a peer-reviewed journal or a Reddit comment.
**Countermeasure:** Meticulous source recording at the moment of discovery using citation management tools that automatically capture metadata.

## 8. Motivated Reasoning
**How it manifests:** Active, goal-directed reasoning while maintaining an "illusion of objectivity." Lord et al. (1979) showed death penalty supporters and opponents both became more extreme after reading identical studies.
**Countermeasure:** Adopt Julia Galef's "scout mindset" — seek to map reality accurately rather than defend a position. Actively seek out the strongest version of opposing arguments.
