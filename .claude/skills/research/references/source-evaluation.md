# Source Evaluation

## Lateral vs. Vertical Reading

**Lateral reading** — leave the page, open new tabs, check what independent sources say about the original. This is what professional fact-checkers do.

**Vertical reading** — stay on the page, scroll down, evaluate based on the page's own content and design. This is what most people do, and it fails against sophisticated misinformation.

### The Stanford Evidence

Wineburg & McGrew (2019, *Teachers College Record*) compared PhD historians, professional fact-checkers, and Stanford undergraduates:
- **100% of fact-checkers** correctly identified a front group vs. a legitimate medical organization
- **50% of historians** succeeded
- **35% of students** got it right (65% chose the hate group's site as more credible)

Key behavioral difference: fact-checkers left unfamiliar sites within ~32 seconds to investigate externally. Historians stayed ~88 seconds. 9/25 students never left the page.

Students and historians were fooled by professional-looking names, logos, scholarly references, and .org domains (anyone can buy a .org domain without vetting).

### Practicing Lateral Reading

1. Leave the unfamiliar page immediately
2. Open new tabs — search for the organization, author, and publisher
3. Read what 3–5 independent sources say about the original
4. If no independent sources discuss it, that absence is informative (source lacks established reputation)
5. Determine consensus, then return with context

**Click restraint** — review search result snippets carefully before clicking, rather than reflexively selecting the top result.

### Empirical Results

13 studies, ~10,000 participants (Caulfield & Wineburg, 2025, *American Educator*):
- <6 hours of lateral reading instruction → significant improvement
- 6× increase in use of fact-checking techniques
- 5× increase in citations of appropriate context

## Framework Comparison

### CRAAP Test (2004, CSU Chico)
**Criteria:** Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose
**Strength:** Concrete guiding questions for each dimension. Most widely adopted in higher education.
**Weakness:** Keeps you on the source (vertical reading). Wineburg et al. (2020) found it makes students *more* susceptible to misinformation because sophisticated disinfo sites pass surface-level checklist criteria.
**Best for:** Evaluating traditional academic sources in library settings.

### RADAR (2013, Mandalios)
**Criteria:** Relevance, Authority, Date, Appearance, Reason for writing
**Strength:** Positions itself as relative quality assessment, not yes/no test. Adds Appearance criterion.
**Weakness:** Same vertical-reading limitation as CRAAP.

### SMELL Test (2013, McManus / Stanford)
**Criteria:** Source, Motivation, Evidence, Logic, Left out
**Strength:** Designed for news/media. "Left Out" criterion (what's missing that might change interpretation) is unique and sophisticated.
**Best for:** Detecting bias in journalism.

### ACT UP (2017, Stahura / Salem State)
**Criteria:** Author, Currency, Truth, Unbiased, Privilege
**Strength:** Centers social justice. Privilege criterion examines who has power to publish and whose voices are excluded.
**Best for:** Challenging citation circles that perpetuate dominant narratives.

### SIFT (2017/2019, Caulfield)
**Criteria:** Stop, Investigate source, Find better coverage, Trace to original
**Strength:** Lateral approach empirically superior for web content. Fast (30–90 seconds). Endorsed by ACRL as offering "major improvements over CRAAP in speed, simplicity, and applicability."
**Weakness:** Less suited for deep evaluation of traditional academic sources where CRAAP may still apply.

### Key Insight

Professional fact-checkers do not use checklists. They spend minimal time on a source's website and quickly investigate externally. SIFT codifies what experts actually do.
