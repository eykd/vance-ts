# Synthesis & Research Ethics

## Synthesis Methods

### Synthesis Matrix
The most powerful technique for building a coherent picture from multiple sources. Structure a table with sources along one axis and themes/research questions along the other. For each cell, note what that source contributes to that theme.

**Reading across rows** reveals how sources relate on the same topic — agreements, disagreements, gaps. **Empty cells** identify under-researched areas. Transforms source-by-source reading into theme-by-theme analysis.

### Zettelkasten Method
Originated by Niklas Luhmann (~90,000 index cards → 70 books, 400 articles). Principles:
- **Atomic notes** — one idea per note, in your own words
- **Explicit links** — connect to related notes
- Digital tools: Obsidian, Roam Research (bidirectional linking)

### Progressive Summarization (Tiago Forte)
Layered approach: save key excerpts → bold the most important passages → highlight critical elements → write a mini-summary. Principle: "just-in-time" processing — add depth only as notes become relevant to active projects.

### Notes-to-Writing Workflow
1. Read sources once to map the landscape
2. Reread to highlight findings relevant to your specific argument
3. Organize notes **by concept**, not by source (this is where synthesis happens)
4. Group concepts into themes → section headings
5. Write from concept notes, not individual source notes
6. Maintain a running note for your overarching thesis

|---|---|---|

## Research Ethics

### Citation & Plagiarism
- Every idea, data point, and quotation must be traced to its original source
- Plagiarism includes inadequate paraphrasing and failure to cite
- AI-generated text used without disclosure constitutes plagiarism
- Use required citation style (APA, MLA, Chicago, etc.)

### Fair Use (17 U.S.C. § 107)
Permits use for criticism, commentary, teaching, and research. Four-factor case-by-case evaluation:
1. Purpose and character of use (transformative vs. duplicative)
2. Nature of the original work
3. Amount used
4. Effect on market for the original

Educational use is **not** automatically fair use.

### Creative Commons Licenses
Standardized conditions: Attribution (BY), Share Alike (SA), No Derivatives (ND), NonCommercial (NC).

### AI-Generated Content
Per Oxford/Cambridge/Copenhagen framework (*Nature Machine Intelligence*, Nov 2024):
1. Human vetting of accuracy and integrity required
2. Substantial human intellectual contribution required
3. Transparent acknowledgment of AI use required

AI tools cannot legally author content. AI outputs may include hallucinated citations with legitimate-looking formatting. **Every AI-generated claim must be verified against primary sources.** When citing AI outputs: include tool name, version, date of use, and prompt.

### Privacy & Digital Footprint
- Search engines log queries; clearing browser history doesn't remove records held by Google, ISPs, or sites
- Research topics become part of your digital profile
- Protections: institutional library databases, VPNs, privacy-focused search engines (DuckDuckGo)
- AI tool inputs are logged and potentially used for training
- When researching public social media data: consider whether subjects would expect their posts to be studied and whether identifying information could cause harm
