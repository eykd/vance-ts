# Octalysis Game Techniques Reference

## Core Drive 1: Epic Meaning & Calling

### Technique #10: Narrative
Use storytelling to create meaning and context for user actions. Connect user behavior to a larger story or purpose.

### Technique #23: Beginner's Luck
Provide early unexpected rewards or success to make new users feel special or "chosen."

### Technique #24: Free Lunch
Give something valuable for free early on, making users feel indebted and invested.

### Technique #26: Elitism
Make users feel they are part of an exclusive group or special class of people.

### Technique #27: Humanity Hero
Frame user actions as helping humanity or making the world a better place.

---

## Core Drive 2: Development & Accomplishment

### Technique #1: Status Points
Numerical representation of progress (XP, reputation points, levels).

### Technique #2: Achievement Symbols
Badges, trophies, medals that represent completed achievements.

### Technique #3: Leaderboards
Rankings showing user performance relative to others.

### Technique #4: Progress Bars
Visual representation of progress toward a goal.

### Technique #92: Rockstar Effect
Public celebration of achievements that makes users feel exceptional.

---

## Core Drive 3: Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback

### Technique #11: Plant Picker - Meaningful Choice
Provide choices that significantly impact outcomes and allow personal expression.

### Technique #19: Milestone Unlock
Unlock new features, content, or abilities as users progress.

### Technique #31: Boosters
Temporary power-ups or enhancements that change how users interact with the system.

### Technique #89: Poison Picker - Choice Perception
Create the perception of meaningful choice even when outcomes are similar.

---

## Core Drive 4: Ownership & Possession

### Technique #16: Collection Sets
Groups of items to collect, encouraging completion behavior.

### Technique #42: Monitor Attachment
Make users track or care for something (virtual pet, garden, profile) that requires attention.

### Technique #43: Build from Scratch
Let users create something from the ground up, increasing ownership feeling.

### Technique #75: Exchangeable Points
Currency or points that can be traded for various rewards.

### Technique #83: Alfred Effect
Personalization features that make the system feel like it belongs to the user.

---

## Core Drive 5: Social Influence & Relatedness

### Technique #22: Group Quests
Tasks or challenges that require cooperation between users.

### Technique #55: Water Coolers
Spaces or features designed for social interaction and casual conversation.

### Technique #57: Brag Buttons
Easy ways for users to share achievements or progress on social media.

### Technique #58: Conformity Anchor
Show what most users are doing to influence behavior through social proof.

### Technique #61: Mentorship
System that pairs experienced users with newcomers.

### Technique #62: Social Prods
Notifications about friends' activities to encourage engagement.

### Technique #63: Social Treasures
Rewards that can only be obtained through social interaction.

### Technique #64: Trophy Shelves
Public display areas where users can show off achievements.

---

## Core Drive 6: Scarcity & Impatience

### Common Techniques:
- Limited-time offers
- Countdown timers
- Appointment dynamics (must return at specific times)
- Throttles (energy systems, stamina)
- Dangling rewards (can see but not yet access)
- Exclusive access windows

---

## Core Drive 7: Unpredictability & Curiosity

### Common Techniques:
- Random rewards (loot boxes, gacha)
- Easter eggs
- Variable reward schedules
- Mystery boxes
- Rolling rewards
- Sudden reveals
- Oracle effect (predictions, horoscopes)
- Glowing choice (mysterious options)

---

## Core Drive 8: Loss & Avoidance

### Common Techniques:
- Progress loss warnings
- FOMO mechanics (fear of missing out)
- Visual graph/streak displays showing potential loss
- Evanescence opportunity (disappearing chances)
- Status quo sloth (harder to change than to stay)
- Sunk cost prison (too invested to quit)
- Scarlet letter (public marking of failure)

---

## Technique Selection Guidelines

### By Lifecycle Phase:

**Discovery:**
- Epic Meaning & Calling techniques (Narrative, Humanity Hero)
- Social Influence techniques (Conformity Anchor, Trophy Shelves)

**Onboarding:**
- Epic Meaning techniques (Beginner's Luck, Free Lunch)
- Development & Accomplishment techniques (Progress Bars, Achievement Symbols)
- Empowerment techniques (Plant Picker, Milestone Unlock)

**Scaffolding:**
- All core drives should be represented
- Balance White Hat and Black Hat
- Strong Ownership techniques (Build from Scratch, Collection Sets)
- Social techniques (Group Quests, Mentorship)

**Endgame:**
- Unpredictability techniques (Random rewards, Easter eggs)
- Social techniques (Mentorship, Trophy Shelves)
- Development techniques (Leaderboards, Rockstar Effect)
- Carefully use Loss & Avoidance techniques

### By Player Type:

**Achievers:**
- Development & Accomplishment techniques
- Ownership techniques
- Clear progression markers

**Explorers:**
- Empowerment of Creativity techniques
- Unpredictability techniques
- Hidden content and Easter eggs

**Socializers:**
- Social Influence techniques
- Group activities
- Communication features

**Killers:**
- Competitive Development techniques (Leaderboards)
- Social Influence techniques (competitive aspects)
- Status and dominance markers

---

## Ethical Considerations

### Healthy Technique Use:
- Transparent mechanics
- User agency and control
- Positive reinforcement emphasis
- Optional social features
- Clear value proposition

### Warning Signs of Manipulation:
- Heavy reliance on Loss & Avoidance
- Hidden costs or mechanics
- Exploitative use of Scarcity
- Dark patterns in choice design
- Predatory social pressure
- Addiction-forming unpredictability patterns

### Guidelines:
1. Prefer White Hat over Black Hat
2. Use Black Hat sparingly and transparently
3. Give users genuine value, not just engagement
4. Respect user time and attention
5. Provide clear exit paths
6. Avoid exploiting cognitive biases for harm
