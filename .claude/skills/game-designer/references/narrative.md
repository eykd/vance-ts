# Narrative Reference

## The Core Tension

Story is linear and authored. Game mechanics are non-linear and emergent. Forced together naively, they fight.

The goal is not to eliminate the tension — it is to design _across_ it, so mechanics and story reinforce each other.

## Three Sources of Narrative Meaning

| Source        | Generated by                  | Player experience          |
| ------------- | ----------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| **World**     | Exploration, discovery        | "I found something"        |
| **Character** | Relationships, transformation | "I became someone"         |
| **Plot**      | Structured event sequences    | "Something happened to me" |

Strong game narratives draw from all three. A game with only plot is a movie. A game with only world is a sandbox. The intersection creates meaning.

## The Story Machine

The best game stories are ones players _tell each other after the session_. These are generated by mechanics, not scripted. Design mechanics that produce shareable stories as a side effect.

Ask: _What stories will players tell about this game in 10 years?_ Design backward from that.

## The Hero's Journey (Campbell/Schell)

Players naturally experience the monomyth in well-designed games. Align progression structure to it:

1. **Ordinary World** — tutorial, low stakes, establish baseline
2. **Call to Adventure** — the inciting mechanic or story hook
3. **Crossing the Threshold** — first irreversible commitment
4. **Trials** — escalating challenges, skill acquisition
5. **Ordeal** — the hardest moment, apparent defeat
6. **Reward** — new power, new understanding
7. **Road Back** — final challenge with full mastery
8. **Return Transformed** — end state, resolution

Games that align level/act structure to this arc feel emotionally satisfying even without conscious analysis.

## Authored vs. Emergent Story

| Type     | Designer controls                  | Player experience |
| -------- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------- |
| Authored | Scripted cutscenes, dialogue trees | Witness           |
| Emergent | Mechanics + systems                | Participant       |
| Hybrid   | Authored beats, emergent between   | Both              |

Most narrative games are hybrid. The craft is knowing _which moments to author_ (emotional peaks, character reveals, world exposition) and _which to leave emergent_ (conflict, exploration, social dynamics).

## Character & World Design

- **Character names**: memorable, often slightly ridiculous, always treated seriously. Names carry implied world.
- **World exposition**: show, don't tell. Invent terms that imply civilizations: _"Tier-3 Clearance"_ implies an entire bureaucracy.
- **Moral order**: does the world have moral gravity, or is morality purely local/performative? Neither is wrong — but it must be intentional.

## Narrative Design Patterns (QBN-adjacent)

For games using state-driven narrative:

- **Gauntlet**: progress quality enforces sequence (Quest_Progress == 3 unlocks next beat)
- **Branch & Bottleneck**: divergent paths converge at a shared event
- **Loop & Grow**: repeating cycles with progression layered over them
- **Menace**: accumulating negative state triggers consequence events (high tension, requires safety valve)
- **Parallel Arcs**: independent storylines with cross-arc interactions at key moments

## Narrative Checklist

- [ ] Does mechanics reinforce or contradict the story's theme?
- [ ] Are there stories players can generate and tell, not just receive?
- [ ] Does the progression arc mirror the Hero's Journey?
- [ ] Are authored moments used for emotional peaks (not exposition dumps)?
- [ ] Does the world have internally consistent moral gravity?
- [ ] Do player choices have narrative consequences, not just mechanical ones?
