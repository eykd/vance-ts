# The Complete Guide to Building High-Converting Landing Pages

## Donald Miller's "Marketing Made Simple" Wireframe Method

---

## Introduction: Why This Framework Works

Most landing pages fail because they make a critical mistake: they talk about themselves instead of their customer. Donald Miller's wireframe approach, outlined in _Marketing Made Simple_, flips this around. It treats your landing page as a story where **your customer is the hero**, and you are the guide helping them overcome their challenges.

This framework works because it follows the psychological journey every buyer takes:

1. "Do you understand my problem?"
2. "Can you actually help me?"
3. "How does this work?"
4. "Can I trust you?"
5. "Is this worth the risk?"
6. "What do I do next?"

Each section of the wireframe answers one of these questions in the right order. Miss a sectionâ€”or get the order wrongâ€”and you lose conversions.

---

## The Core Principle: Clarity, Not Cleverness

Before diving into the sections, burn this into your brain:

> **Clear beats clever. Every time.**

When visitors land on your page, they're asking: _"What is this, and how does it help me?"_ If they can't answer that question within 5 seconds, they're gone.

Avoid:

- Insider jargon your customers don't use
- Vague aspirational language ("Unlock your potential")
- Clever wordplay that confuses more than it impresses

Use:

- Simple words (8th-grade reading level)
- Specific benefits ("Save 10 hours per week")
- Direct language that anyone could understand

## The Complete Wireframe: Section by Section

### Section 1: The Header (Hero Section)

**Purpose:** Capture attention immediately and answer three questions:

1. What do you offer?
2. How will it make my life better?
3. What do I need to do to get it?

**Elements:**

- Logo (top left)
- Minimal navigation (top right, 3-5 items maximum)
- Headline (large, central, commanding)
- Sub-headline (clarifies and expands on the promise)
- Primary call-to-action button
- Hero image or visual (shows success, not process)

#### The Headline

Your headline is the single most important element on the page. It must communicate your value proposition in 6-10 words or fewer.

**Headline Formula:**

> [End Result] + [Timeframe or Method] = Compelling Headline

**Examples of Strong Headlines:**

- "Ship Your Startup in Hours, Not Weeks"
- "Grow Your Business With Less Stress"
- "Double Your Sales in 90 Days"
- "Beautiful Websites That Convert Visitors to Customers"

**Examples of Weak Headlines:**

- "Welcome to Our Company" (says nothing)
- "The Future of Innovation" (too vague)
- "Synergistic Solutions for Dynamic Enterprises" (meaningless jargon)

#### The Sub-headline

Your sub-headline should clarify and expand on your headline. It's where you can get slightly more specific about how you deliver on your promise.

**Format:** 1-2 sentences that:

- Explain _how_ you deliver the result promised in your headline
- Eliminate a specific pain point
- Set expectations for what happens next

**Example:**

- Headline: "Ship Your Startup in Hours, Not Weeks"
- Sub-headline: "The Python boilerplate with everything includedâ€”user auth, payments, deploymentâ€”so you can focus on building your actual product."

#### The Call-to-Action (CTA)

Your CTA button should tell visitors exactly what happens when they click. Make it specific and action-oriented.

**Strong CTAs:**

- "Purchase Now"
- "Start Your Free Trial"
- "Schedule Your Call"
- "Get Your Custom Quote"
- "Download the Free Guide"

**Weak CTAs:**

- "Submit" (submit what?)
- "Learn More" (too vague)
- "Click Here" (meaningless)

#### The Hero Image

Your image should show the **result**, not the process. Show a person experiencing the transformation you offerâ€”not a picture of your product sitting alone.

- âœ“ Happy customer using your product successfully
- âœ“ Visual metaphor for freedom, success, achievement
- âœ— Screenshot of your software interface
- âœ— Stock photo of random smiling people

### Section 2: The Stakes

**Purpose:** Create tension by demonstrating that you understand the customer's problem and showing the cost of inaction.

This is where many landing pages fail. They rush straight to their solution without first establishing _why the customer should care_. The stakes section proves you understand your customer's world.

**Elements:**

- Headline framed as a question or statement about their problem
- 3-4 bullet points describing specific challenges they face
- Brief empathy statement acknowledging their frustration

#### The Problem Headline

Frame the problem as a question that your ideal customer would answer "yes" to:

**Examples:**

- "Are You Struggling With Marketing That Doesn't Work?"
- "Tired of Losing Momentum on Every New Project?"
- "Does Your Business Feel Like It's Stuck?"

#### The Pain Points

List 3-4 specific, concrete challenges. Be preciseâ€”vague problems don't resonate.

**Strong Pain Points:**

- "You're spending money on ads but not seeing results"
- "You've started three projects this year and finished none"
- "Every time you sit down to code, you waste hours on setup"

**Weak Pain Points:**

- "Things are hard" (too vague)
- "You're not reaching your potential" (empty language)
- "Marketing is challenging" (says nothing specific)

#### The Empathy Statement

Close this section by acknowledging their frustration. Show that you _get it_.

**Example:**

> "We know how frustrating it is to pour your energy into something only to watch it stall out. You're working too hard to keep hitting the same walls."

#### Why the Stakes Section Matters

Psychologically, people are more motivated to avoid pain than to seek pleasure. By clearly articulating the stakesâ€”what they lose by _not_ actingâ€”you create urgency. You're saying: _"The status quo is not acceptable."_

### Section 3: The Value Proposition

**Purpose:** Present your solution and the transformation it provides.

Now that you've established the problem, it's time to position yourself as the solution. But here's the key: **talk about benefits, not features**.

**Elements:**

- Section headline
- 3 key benefits (with icons or images)
- Brief description for each benefit
- Each benefit connects back to a pain point from Section 2

#### Features vs. Benefits

**Features** are what your product does.  
**Benefits** are what your customer gets.

| "100% test coverage" | "Deploy with confidenceâ€”no bugs slipping through"  |
| "Stripe integration" | "Start accepting payments on day one"              |

Always lead with the benefit. Features are proof that supports the benefit.

#### The Three-Benefit Structure

Why three? Because three is memorable. Three is scannable. Three forces you to prioritize.

**Structure each benefit like this:**

1. **Benefit Headline** (3-5 words): "Save Hours Every Week"
2. **Description** (2-3 sentences): Explain the transformation. Use "you" language. Connect back to the pain point.

### Section 4: The Guide

**Purpose:** Build trust by positioning yourself as a credible, empathetic authority.

This is where you introduce yourselfâ€”but not with a self-indulgent bio. You're demonstrating two qualities:

1. **Empathy:** "I understand your struggle."
2. **Authority:** "I have the expertise to help."

Think of yourself as the mentor in a story. Your customer is Luke Skywalker. You are Obi-Wan Kenobi.

**Elements:**

- Brief statement of credentials/experience
- Social proof (testimonials, logos, numbers)
- Empathy statement showing you've walked in their shoes

#### Demonstrating Empathy

Share your own struggle with the problem. This creates connection.

**Example:**

> "I've started and abandoned more projects than I can count. Every time, I'd get bogged down in the same boring setup workâ€”user auth, password reset, database configuration. By the time I got to the actual product code, my excitement had faded. Sound familiar?"

Once you've shown empathy, establish your credentials. But keep it relevantâ€”only mention experience that proves you can solve _their_ problem.

**Strong Authority Statements:**

- "With 15 years building high-performance web applications in Python, I've learned what works."
- "We've helped 500+ companies launch their MVPs."
- "Our framework handles 10 million requests per day across our clients' apps."

**Weak Authority Statements:**

- "I have a passion for excellence" (meaningless)
- "We're an industry-leading provider" (vague)

#### Social Proof

Include concrete evidence that others trust you:

- **Numbers:** "10,000+ customers served"
- **Logos:** Display recognizable client/partner logos
- **Testimonials:** Short, specific quotes with real names
- **Results:** "Helped clients generate $10M in revenue"
- **Media:** "As seen in Forbes, TechCrunch, etc."

### Section 5: The Plan

**Purpose:** Make taking action feel simple and achievable.

Customers don't buy when they're confused. The plan section removes confusion by showing exactly what happens after they click the button.

**Elements:**

- Section headline ("Here's How It Works" or "The Simple Path to [Result]")
- 3 numbered steps (occasionally 4, never more)
- Clear, actionable language
- Step 1 is easy; Step 3 is about _their_ success

#### The Three-Step Formula

**Step 1: Make It Easy**
The first step should feel low-risk and effortless. This is where they dip their toe in.

- "Schedule a free call"
- "Sign up in 30 seconds"
- "Download the guide"

**Step 2: Show Your Role**
The middle step shows what happens after they take action. You're doing work for them.

- "We create your custom plan"
- "You get the complete framework delivered to your inbox"
- "Our team configures everything for you"

**Step 3: Their Success**
The final step is about _their_ transformationâ€”not your delivery.

- âœ“ "Watch your business grow"
- âœ“ "Launch your product to customers"
- âœ— "We complete the project" (this is about _you_, not them)

**Example:**

> **Get Based. It's Simple:**

> **1. Buy the Boilerplate**  
> Upon purchase, you'll have instant access to the whole package and a license to use it for unlimited sites.

> **2. Add Your Code & Deploy**

> **3. Launch to Customers and Iterate**

### Section 6: The Explanatory Paragraph (Long-Form Copy)

**Purpose:** Provide details for visitors who need more information before deciding.

Not everyone converts after scanning headlines. Some people want the full story. This section is for them.

**This is your chance to:**

- Tell a relatable story
- Expand on the transformation
- Address objections proactively
- Use concrete, visual language

#### The Persuasive Copy Framework

1. **State the Goal:** Who does your customer want to become?
2. **Get Attention:** Tell a quick story with vivid language.
3. **Demonstrate Need:** Define the problem setting them back.
4. **Offer Satisfaction:** Position yourself as the guide.
5. **Lay Out the Plan:** How will they succeed with your help?
6. **Overcome Objections:** Address concerns about cost, time, risk.
7. **Cast a Vision:** What does their life look like after success?
8. **Call to Action:** Tell them exactly what to do next.

#### Writing Tips for Long-Form Copy

- **Use short paragraphs.** 2-3 sentences maximum. This is "internet style."
- **Break up text with subheadings.** Guide skimmers to the parts that matter.
- **Be conversational.** Write like you're talking to one person.
- **Use "you" language.** Make it about them.
- **Tell a story.** Stories are memorable. Data is forgettable.
- **Be specific.** "Save 10 hours per week" beats "save time."

### Section 8: Pricing

**Purpose:** Present your offer clearly and make it easy to choose.

**The Three-Tier Strategy:**

Most landing pages should present three pricing options:

1. **Budget Tier (Left):** Entry-level option for price-sensitive buyers. Limited features, lower price.

2. **Standard Tier (Center):** Your "meat and potatoes" option. This is where most customers should land. Make it visually prominent.

3. **Premium Tier (Right):** High-end option for customers who want the best. Higher price acts as an anchor, making the middle tier look reasonable.

**Why Three Tiers Work:**

- Few people want the cheapest (feels inferior)
- Few people want the most expensive (feels excessive)
- Most people choose the middle (feels balanced)

The premium tier's job isn't necessarily to sellâ€”it's to make your standard tier look like a great deal.

**Each Tier Should Include:**

- Clear name that signals who it's for
- Price displayed prominently
- Bullet list of what's included
- CTA button
- Risk reversal (guarantee, free trial, etc.)

**Risk Reversal: The Trust Accelerator**

Offering a guarantee dramatically increases conversions because it removes fear.

Types of risk reversal:

- **Money-back guarantee:** "30-day no-questions-asked refund"
- **Free trial:** "Try free for 14 days"
- **Results guarantee:** "If you don't see X result in Y days, we'll Z"

### Section 10: FAQ (Frequently Asked Questions)

**Purpose:** Address objections and eliminate reasons not to buy.

**Common Objection Categories:**

3. **Risk:** "What if it doesn't work?"
4. **Price:** "Is it worth the investment?"
5. **Timing:** "Why should I do this now?"

**FAQ Best Practices:**

- 5-8 questions maximum
- Keep answers concise (2-4 sentences)
- Address the objection directlyâ€”don't dodge

## Design Principles

### Visual Hierarchy

Not all elements are equally important. Your design should guide eyes to what matters:

1. **Headline** (largest, most prominent)
2. **CTA buttons** (contrasting color, impossible to miss)
3. **Supporting copy** (readable but not competing with headline)
4. **Details** (smaller, secondary)

### Whitespace Is Your Friend

Cluttered pages overwhelm visitors. Give your content room to breathe. When in doubt, add more whitespace.

### Mobile-First

60%+ of your traffic is mobile. Design for mobile first, then adapt for desktop:

- Single-column layout on mobile
- Minimum 16px body text
- Minimum 44px touch targets for buttons
- Sticky header or CTA for easy access
- Reduce text by 30-40% for mobile versions

### CTA Placement

Include a call-to-action every 1-2 scrolls. Visitors should never have to search for the button.

1. **Focusing on features instead of benefits**
   - âœ— "We have 47 features"
   - âœ“ "You'll save 10 hours every week"

2. **Being clever instead of clear**
   - âœ— "Unlock your synergistic potential"

   - âœ— "We're the industry leader"
   - âœ“ "Grow your business with less stress"

4. **Multiple competing CTAs**
   - Pick ONE primary action per page

5. **Skipping the stakes section**
   - If they don't feel the problem, they won't want the solution

6. **Generic testimonials**
   - "Great service!" means nothing
   - Get specific results and stories

7. **Hiding pricing**

   - Every link away from the page is a lost conversion

9. **No mobile optimization**
   - Test on actual devices

10. **Forgetting the guarantee**
    - Risk reversal dramatically increases conversions

## Testing and Optimization

Your first landing page is a hypothesis. Test and iterate.

### What to A/B Test (In Order of Impact)

1. **Headlines** (biggest impact)
2. **CTA button copy and color**
3. **Hero images**
4. **Pricing presentation**
5. **Testimonial placement**

### Key Metrics to Track

- **Conversion rate** (primary metric)
- Bounce rate
- Time on page
- Scroll depth
- Click-through rate on CTAs

### The Optimization Cycle

1. Launch with best practices
2. Gather data (minimum 100 conversions or 30 days)
3. Identify lowest-performing sections
4. Form hypothesis for improvement
5. Test ONE change at a time
6. Measure results
7. Implement winner
8. Repeat

## Final Thoughts

Donald Miller's wireframe works because it follows a clear psychological sequence:

1. **Hook them** with a clear promise (header)
2. **Show you understand** their problem (stakes)
3. **Present your solution** clearly (value proposition)
4. **Build trust** with credentials (guide)
5. **Simplify the process** (plan)
6. **Provide details** for those who need them (explanatory)
7. **Prove it works** (testimonials)
8. **Make it easy to say yes** (pricing/offer)
9. **Remove objections** (FAQ)
10. **Close strong** (final CTA)

Every section answers a question your visitor is asking. Miss a question, and you lose the conversion.

Remember: **A landing page is a conversation with your customer.** Every section should feel like a natural response to what they're thinking as they scroll.

And above all: **Clarity beats cleverness. Every time.**
