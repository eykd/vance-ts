## Key Findings (chronological order)

### [2006] -- "Chu Chu Carnage" Theory of Emergent Plot

- **Source**: 2006-06-09-emergent-rather-than-generative-plot.md (qmd://perko/2006-06-09-emergent-rather-than-generative-plot.md)
- **Date**: 2006-06-09
- **Key idea**: Perko draws a sharp distinction between "emergent" and "generative" plot. Generative = computer builds the story algorithmically. Emergent = simple rules create complex situations that the player (and NPCs) interpret as plot. He proposes the "chu chu carnage" model: NPCs are carefully-placed "components" on a social field (like The Incredible Machine), each with simple behavioral rules. The player tweaks components, causing system-wide cascading reactions -- and those cascading reactions _are_ the plot.
- **Notable quote**: "The idea is that the components are carefully placed so that there is a system-wide reaction when the player changes something significant, and that system-wide reaction _is a plot_."
- **Notable quote 2**: "Instead of designing a plot tree, you design a 'plot level'." (from comments)
- **Connection to yarnball narrative philosophy**: This is the earliest clear articulation of what becomes the yarnball idea -- narrative as an emergent property of interconnected systems rather than a scripted tree. The "components on a field" metaphor prefigures yarnball's "everything connected to everything" topology. The key insight is the same: individual elements are simple, but their interconnections produce narrative complexity.

### [2006] -- Characters as Travelers, Not Backdrops

- **Source**: 2006-10-11-generative-stories-pain.md (qmd://perko/2006-10-11-generative-stories-pain.md)
- **Date**: 2006-10-11
- **Key idea**: Perko argues that traditional game characters are either locked to locations (backdrops) or completely separate from them (party members). Real stories require characters who _travel between_ locations and interact meaningfully with each one. He proposes borrowing from LARP design: characters act _in character_ regardless of context. The world itself should be generated as needed for the story, not pre-designed.
- **Notable quote**: "Don't think of characters in terms of where they can be found. And don't think of characters as separate from where they are. Think of characters as travelling from location to location, and interacting meaningfully with every location, every event, every person."
- **Notable quote 2**: "That's what a story IS."
- **Connection to yarnball narrative philosophy**: This post establishes that narrative emerges from the _movement_ and _interaction_ of characters through a space -- not from static placement. The yarnball concept requires elements that connect dynamically rather than being pre-wired. Perko is already thinking about how systems create meaning through traversal and contact.

### [2007] -- Component Plots: Big Atoms, Small Atoms, and Player-Generated Context

- **Source**: 2007-08-14-component-plots.md (qmd://perko/2007-08-14-component-plots.md)
- **Date**: 2007-08-14
- **Key idea**: Perko taxonomizes approaches to generative narrative by "atom size": big atoms (heavily scripted branches, e.g., KOTOR), small atoms (tiny generic fragments that produce gibberish), medium atoms (Diablo-style chunks). None solve the "context problem" -- a computer cannot understand narrative context. He proposes three solutions: (1) player-generated context (supply atoms, let players assemble meaning), (2) level-generated context (a "map" of the plot filled with context-suited chunks), (3) "long atoms" that cast backward (foreshadowing) and forward (repercussions). In comments, he discusses juxtaposing scripted fragments to change perceived meaning without new writing.
- **Notable quote**: "If you supply tiny story atoms to players, they will be happy to assemble them into fully functional plots that make sense."
- **Notable quote 2** (from comments): "What I was thinking of was not nearly so random: it has a strong progression from start to finish, but the developing story can create a fairly large number of meaningful contexts using randomly selected bits of script... so although the story is fundamentally the same, the user's perception of it varies wildly."
- **Connection to yarnball narrative philosophy**: The "long atoms" concept -- elements that cast connections both forward and backward in time -- is proto-yarnball thinking. It describes narrative elements as nodes with temporal connections, not linear sequences. The idea that meaning changes through juxtaposition rather than explicit scripting is central to how yarnball narratives create emergent meaning.

### [2008] -- Roguelikes and the Limits of Self-Generated Narrative

- **Source**: 2008-02-29-hey-let-s-talk-roguelikes.md (qmd://perko/2008-02-29-hey-let-s-talk-roguelikes.md)
- **Date**: 2008-02-29
- **Key idea**: Perko articulates why he finds roguelikes unsatisfying despite their emergent properties. He values "externality" -- the sense of exploring someone else's carefully constructed creative vision, where narrative resonates with the game engine. He acknowledges player-generated narratives exist in roguelikes (cites Dwarf Fortress) but finds them insufficient. A narrative must be "built on, around, in, and with the rules and the content."
- **Notable quote**: "It is a story that resonates with the game engine: a story built on, around, in, and with the rules and the content. It wouldn't be the same seen on TV, because the depth would be missing."
- **Connection to yarnball narrative philosophy**: This establishes Perko's conviction that emergent narrative alone is not enough -- the system must be _designed_ to produce narratively rich emergence. Pure randomness produces stories only through player imagination. The yarnball model requires deliberate architectural choices (what connects to what) to ensure emergent stories have coherence and depth.

### [2009] -- The Outer Ring: Narrative as the Outermost Gameplay Loop

- **Source**: 2009-01-24-the-outer-ring.md (qmd://perko/2009-01-24-the-outer-ring.md)
- **Date**: 2009-01-24
- **Key idea**: Perko identifies nested gameplay loops and argues that compelling games need a textured "outer ring" -- the outermost loop that gives long-term meaning. For many games, this is the narrative. For construction games (Sim City), it is the cumulative effect of your own decisions. Games he dislikes (GTA, Spelunky) have an outer ring that "spins freely" without texture or meaningful progression.
- **Notable quote**: "Usually the outermost play loop has something to say. For example, it's usually the narrative of the game."
- **Notable quote 2** (from comments): "In games like Sim City or similar, the outermost loop is built BY YOU as you play the game: your prior decisions begin to affect your current situation in a dramatic and complex way."
- **Connection to yarnball narrative philosophy**: The outer ring concept is the structural demand that yarnball narrative fills. Yarnball is an architecture for making the outermost gameplay loop textured and emergent simultaneously -- neither purely authored (which limits replayability) nor purely random (which lacks meaning). It gives the outer ring "something to say" through systemic interconnection.

### [2013] -- War Stories: XCom-Style Emergent Narrative from Tactical Mechanics

- **Source**: 2013-04-25-war-stories-and-power-rangers.md (qmd://perko/2013-04-25-war-stories-and-power-rangers.md)
- **Date**: 2013-04-25
- **Key idea**: Perko analyzes how XCom generates compelling "war stories" through tactical mechanics. Characters' battlefield actions (holding position, taking hits, last-second kills) become narrative beats in the player's mind. He then maps this onto Power Rangers' story structure, finding the same pattern: maneuver elements + engagement elements = emergent narrative. He proposes a "tagging" system where units tag allies/enemies to create topology, generating the same narrative richness as spatial tactics but in abstract social space.
- **Notable quote**: "The way that your characters operated in combat was easy to turn into stories in your head, as your badass Rambo held off half a dozen monsters against all odds before dying to a brain-eater. War stories."
- **Notable quote 2**: "In the end, the emergent topology and complex 'who is teamed up at the moment' mechanics should result in the same kind of stories that emerge from games like X-Com."
- **Connection to yarnball narrative philosophy**: This is the clearest bridge between systems mechanics and narrative emergence in Perko's pre-2019 writing. The "tagging" system -- where narrative meaning emerges from who-is-connected-to-whom topology -- is a direct precursor to yarnball's web-of-connections model. The insight that abstract relationship topology produces the same narrative quality as concrete spatial topology is foundational.

### [2018] -- Simple Mechanics for Compelling Games: Anatomy of Emergent Narrative

- **Source**: 2018-01-12-simple-mechanics-for-compelling-games.md (qmd://perko/2018-01-12-simple-mechanics-for-compelling-games.md)
- **Date**: 2018-01-12
- **Key idea**: Perko dissects how even match-3 games produce compelling emergent narratives, identifying four elements: (1) contextual elements (known characters/items that give meaning to mechanical events), (2) endings (how the climax unfolds, not just win/lose), (3) midgame management (depth layered onto simple mechanics), (4) mix-ins (separate gameplay layers woven into the core loop that add narrative beats). He argues any simple gameplay can generate emergent narrative if these elements are present.
- **Notable quote**: "Regardless of anything else, we want our gameplay to allow for that kind of emergent narrative. How do we do that?"
- **Notable quote 2**: "It might be really interesting to try to create an RPG that's been re-engineered from scratch to focus on in-battle emergent narratives."
- **Connection to yarnball narrative philosophy**: Written just one year before the yarnball concept, this post is the most mature pre-yarnball articulation. The four elements (context, endings, management, mix-ins) describe what a yarnball system needs to deliver. The insight that narrative density comes from _layering context onto simple mechanics_ rather than from complex scripting is the direct philosophical foundation of yarnball design.

### [2018] -- Inhabitant-Centric Design: People as the Focus of Emergent Story

- **Source**: 2018-01-11-designing-inhabitant-centric-games.md (qmd://perko/2018-01-11-designing-inhabitant-centric-games.md)
- **Date**: 2018-01-11
- **Key idea**: Perko designs a person-centric base-building game (Sims meets Rimworld) where the purpose of the space is to "help build personal stories both in gameplay and in the player's mind." Dense spaces with variable publicness and contextual room types (bathrooms, bedrooms, public spaces) generate stories through familiar social rules. Inhabitants relating to space with context is the key design goal.
- **Notable quote**: "The purpose of this space is to help build personal stories both in gameplay and in the player's mind."
- **Connection to yarnball narrative philosophy**: This post operationalizes the yarnball philosophy for base-building: design the space and its rules so that inhabitant interactions naturally produce stories the player recognizes and cares about. The focus on "context" -- familiar social situations that players can interpret -- is the same principle that makes yarnball narrative legible rather than random.

## Gaps & Limitations

1. **"Yarnball" term absent pre-2019**: The word "yarnball" does not appear in the collection before 2019. The concept evolved through different metaphors: "chu chu carnage" (2006), "component plots" / "long atoms" (2007), "outer ring" (2009), "tagging topology" (2013). The genealogy is clear but the naming is a late development.

2. **Fallen London / Storylet systems**: Perko does not discuss Fallen London, storylets, or quality-based narrative anywhere in the collection. If the yarnball concept was influenced by storylet design, that influence is not documented in his blog writing.

3. **vsearch unavailable**: Semantic vector search failed due to a model download error (65% of documents lack embeddings). BM25 keyword search was used exclusively. Some relevant posts using different vocabulary may have been missed.

4. **2014-2017 gap**: There is a noticeable thinning of directly relevant posts between the 2013 "War Stories" post and the January 2018 cluster. This period may contain relevant thinking under different terminology.

5. **2019 yarnball post itself**: This search focused on pre-2019 precursors. The 2019 post that formally introduces the "yarnball" concept was not fetched or analyzed here -- that would be a logical next step for completing the genealogy.

6. **Patrick (commenter)**: A recurring commenter named Patrick engages deeply with Perko's narrative ideas across multiple years (2006-2009), consistently pushing toward "emergent" approaches. This dialogue may have shaped Perko's thinking, but the commenter's identity and contributions deserve separate investigation.
