## Key Findings (chronological order)

### [2005] -- Pattern Adaptation Control: The "Clockwork" Metaphor

- **Source**: 2005-12-25-part-three-pieces-of-the-puzzle.md (qmd://perko/2005-12-25-part-three-pieces-of-the-puzzle.md)
- **Date**: 2005-12-25
- **Key idea that prefigures the yarnball**: Perko describes two approaches to adaptive game content: "jewelry box" (pre-crafted elements chosen based on player preference) and "clockwork" (components assembled on the fly into new patterns). The clockwork model -- where simple components combine to create emergent complexity based on player choices -- is a proto-yarnball concept.
- **Notable quote**: "You create a cog that can be plunked into virtually any character that makes them... edgier. Obviously, this is a very adaptable situation, but it is also quite difficult."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: The clockwork model anticipates the yarnball's core idea of modular game components that interlock to create emergent narrative. The key difference is that in 2005 Perko is thinking about designer-authored content adapting to player preference, not player-facing survival systems generating narrative from their interactions.

### [2006] -- Emergent Rather than Generative Plot

- **Source**: 2006-06-09-emergent-rather-than-generative-plot.md (qmd://perko/2006-06-09-emergent-rather-than-generative-plot.md)
- **Date**: 2006-06-09
- **Key idea that prefigures the yarnball**: Perko explicitly distinguishes "emergent" from "generative" plot. He proposes the "chu chu carnage" theory: NPCs as components on a field, where player tweaks cause "system-wide reactions" that _are_ the plot. This is the earliest clear articulation of the principle that systems interactions should produce narrative.
- **Notable quote**: "The point is that you don't have a bunch of largely interchangeable NPCs. You have NPCs which are carefully placed and designed so that they have the potential to create a fun plot... that system-wide reaction _is a plot_."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: Direct ancestor. The 2019 yarnball applies this same principle (systems creating narrative through interactions) specifically to survival mechanics and ship customization. The 2006 version targets social/NPC systems; the 2019 version generalizes it to resource/survival meters.

### [2007] -- The Importance of Attrition

- **Source**: 2007-08-27-the-importance-of-attrition.md (qmd://perko/2007-08-27-the-importance-of-attrition.md)
- **Date**: 2007-08-27
- **Key idea that prefigures the yarnball**: Perko argues that resource attrition (gradual drain) is superior to binary pass/fail challenges. System Shock 2's slow resource drain creates tension and fear; Bioshock's binary "you die or you don't" design eliminates it. This is the earliest articulation of "soft" vs "hard" challenge -- a core yarnball property.
- **Notable quote**: "No given situation will kill you, unless you do something completely silly... But as you go from room to room, you'll continually be hurt _a little_, and that really starts to add up."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: The yarnball explicitly requires "soft, complicated challenges" rather than binary pass/fail. The 2007 attrition post establishes this principle in the context of survival horror. The 2019 version applies it to construction/survival games.

### [2013] -- Aesthetics of Player-Created Starships

- **Source**: 2013-10-09-aesthetics-of-player-created-starships.md (qmd://perko/2013-10-09-aesthetics-of-player-created-starships.md)
- **Date**: 2013-10-09
- **Key idea that prefigures the yarnball**: Perko argues that functional ship components should drive aesthetic design, and that technology rules should give rise to unique in-universe designs. He proposes that ship surface customization (painting crew quarters vs weapons vs science) should directly affect gameplay stats, creating a tight loop between construction choices and gameplay consequences.
- **Notable quote**: "Modules don't allow for the seamless customization ships should have."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: The 2019 Space Survival post uses exactly this ship-customization framework -- ripping out a gym to install a media console, replacing gun hardpoints with subspace antennae -- as the primary mechanism through which survival yarnballs become narrative. The 2013 post establishes the design vocabulary of functional ship customization.

### [2015a] -- Basebuilding With People: Binary Challenges and Disaster Recovery

- **Source**: 2015-02-23-basebuilding-with-people.md (qmd://perko/2015-02-23-basebuilding-with-people.md)
- **Date**: 2015-02-23
- **Key idea that prefigures the yarnball**: Perko explicitly criticizes binary challenges in base-building games: "the challenges RimWorld introduces are usually 'nasty things trying to kill you'. This is a really boring kind of challenge because it's very binary: you tend to either win handily or get annihilated." He identifies disaster recovery and medical repair as more interesting because they are "something you weather, not something you defeat."
- **Notable quote**: "Even the basic challenge of the ship crashing down around you is fundamentally interesting, because it's something you weather, not something you defeat. If you lose people or resources or whole buildings, you can be assured that you can come out of the other side anyway."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: This is the most direct pre-yarnball statement of principle. "Something you weather, not something you defeat" is essentially the definition of a yarnball challenge. The 2019 posts formalize this insight into the yarnball concept and apply it to survival meters.

### [2015b] -- Mission-Based Iteration vs Survival Mode: The Binary Divide Critique

- **Source**: 2015-05-18-mission-based-iteration-vs-survival-mode.md (qmd://perko/2015-05-18-mission-based-iteration-vs-survival-mode.md)
- **Date**: 2015-05-18
- **Key idea that prefigures the yarnball**: Perko criticizes the binary split between "survival mode" (eat or die) and "creative mode" (no constraints) in construction games. He argues challenges should exert "continual pressure" that drives iterative construction improvement, rather than being tick-box obstacles you flatten away.
- **Notable quote**: "The big problem with construction games is that their creative mode and their survival modes are too chunky, too binary, too separate."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: The yarnball concept is precisely the solution to this problem. Instead of binary survival/creative split, the yarnball integrates challenge and construction into a single soft, interlocking system. Perko names the problem in 2015 but doesn't yet have the solution vocabulary ("yarnball") until 2019.

### [2017] -- Base-Building Games and Worlds: Multiple Approaches to One Problem

- **Source**: 2017-06-12-base-building-games-and-worlds.md (qmd://perko/2017-06-12-base-building-games-and-worlds.md)
- **Date**: 2017-06-12
- **Key idea that prefigures the yarnball**: Analyzing Oxygen Not Included, Perko identifies five distinct approaches to any base-building challenge (point-centric, world-centric, scout-centric, physics-centric, evasion). The key insight is that offering multiple approaches to the same pressure creates "amazingly deep gameplay that never repeats" -- because each player's base develops uniquely from their choices.
- **Notable quote**: "There is no 'right' solution: which tactic you'll take will depend on your goals, your resources, and your world map. This makes for a very flexible, deep game."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: The five-approach framework is the analytical backbone of the yarnball. A yarnball works because the same challenge (e.g., "provide air") can be approached multiple ways, each of which interlocks differently with other systems. This post provides the taxonomy; the 2019 posts provide the metaphor and the narrative framing.

### [2018] -- Simple Mechanics for Compelling Games: Emergent Narrative from Simple Rules

- **Source**: 2018-01-12-simple-mechanics-for-compelling-games.md (qmd://perko/2018-01-12-simple-mechanics-for-compelling-games.md)
- **Date**: 2018-01-12
- **Key idea that prefigures the yarnball**: Perko analyzes how match-3 games (Marvel Puzzle Quest, Battle Chef Brigade) generate compelling emergent narratives from simple mechanics. He identifies four components: contextual elements, endings, midgame management, and mix-ins. He proposes that "simple gameplay to make compelling games" works by layering meaning onto basic mechanics.
- **Notable quote**: "Regardless of anything else, we want our gameplay to allow for that kind of emergent narrative. How do we do that?"
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: This post is the immediate theoretical predecessor. It establishes that emergent narrative can arise from simple, interlocking systems -- the yarnball posts apply this to construction/survival specifically. The "mix-in" concept (secondary game systems woven through primary play) directly maps to how survival meters weave through ship customization in the 2019 design.

### [2019a] -- Boiling the Yarnball: First Formal Statement

- **Source**: 2019-06-11-boiling-the-yarnball.md (qmd://perko/2019-06-11-boiling-the-yarnball.md)
- **Date**: 2019-06-11
- **Key idea**: First use of the "yarnball" metaphor. Defines it as "big, soft, messy challenges that can be approached in a lot of different ways with a lot of different entanglements to the rest of the game." Introduces the four phases: planning, construction, daily life, spotlight. Uses The Sims, Dwarf Fortress, and Oxygen Not Included as examples.
- **Notable quote**: "I think it's yarnballs. Big, soft, messy challenges that can be approached in a lot of different ways with a lot of different entanglements to the rest of the game."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: This IS the first yarnball post. The Space Survival post (2019-06-18) applies it one week later.

### [2019b] -- Topological Construction: The Formal Framework

- **Source**: 2019-08-13-topological-construction-play-with-scenarios.md (qmd://perko/2019-08-13-topological-construction-play-with-scenarios.md)
- **Date**: 2019-08-13
- **Key idea**: Formalizes the yarnball's construction aspect into a three-part framework: the mechanic (local effect with consequences), the load (how the mechanic expresses locally), and scenario modules (elements the player installs to face specific challenges). Explicitly states the mechanic "shouldn't be pass-fail: the whole point is to create a soft, widely interlocking mechanic."
- **Notable quote**: "It shouldn't be pass-fail: the whole point is to create a soft, widely interlocking mechanic that the player can adjust in a lot of ways."
- **Connection to 2019 yarnball**: Post-yarnball formalization. Refines the concept into an implementable design pattern.

## Evolution Timeline

The yarnball concept developed across roughly four phases over 14 years:

**Phase 1: Emergence and Adaptation (2005-2006).** Perko explores how game systems can produce emergent narrative. The 2005 "pattern adaptation" essays describe modular game components that combine based on player preferences -- a designer-side version of what later becomes the player-facing yarnball. The 2006 "Emergent Rather than Generative Plot" post is the key early statement: NPCs as interacting components whose system-wide cascades ARE the narrative. The idea of narrative arising from system interactions rather than authored scripts is firmly established.

**Phase 2: Soft vs Binary Challenge (2007-2013).** Perko develops the distinction between gradual pressure (attrition) and binary pass/fail, primarily through analyzing System Shock 2 vs Bioshock (2007). Simultaneously, he explores functional ship customization where construction choices directly affect gameplay (2013 starships). These two threads -- soft challenge and meaningful construction -- are the two halves of the yarnball that have not yet been joined.

**Phase 3: Construction Games and the Binary Critique (2015-2017).** Playing RimWorld and analyzing base-builders, Perko explicitly names the problems the yarnball will solve. Binary challenges are "boring" (2015a). The creative/survival mode split is "too chunky, too binary" (2015b). Multiple approaches to the same pressure create deep, non-repeating gameplay (2017 ONI analysis). He has all the component insights but lacks the unifying metaphor.

**Phase 4: Crystallization (2018-2019).** The 2018 "Simple Mechanics" post shows how emergent narrative arises from layered simple systems, establishing the theoretical framework. In June 2019, the metaphor arrives: "Boiling the Yarnball" names and defines the concept, followed one week later by "Space Survival Gameplay" which applies it to the specific domain of spaceship survival games. The August 2019 "Topological Construction" post formalizes it into an implementable design pattern.
