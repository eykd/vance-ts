# Research Briefing: Craig Perko's Sci-Fi Game Design Insights

**Date**: 2026-03-23
**Research question**: What are Craig Perko's key insights on sci-fi game design, focusing on: space survival mechanics, CRPG design patterns, location/world design, and NPC interaction systems?

## Executive Summary

Craig Perko's blog archive (2005--2019) contains a remarkably coherent design philosophy centered on one meta-principle: **systems should produce emergent narrative from interlocking mechanics, not from scripted content**. Across all four search angles -- space survival, CRPG design, world design, and NPC interaction -- Perko consistently argues that the designer's job is to create _pressure gradients_ and _interconnected meters_ that force players into meaningful tradeoffs, then let the resulting choices tell a story the designer never explicitly wrote.

For a text-only MMORPG with QBN mechanics, Perko's most actionable ideas cluster around three pillars: (1) the "yarnball" survival model, where Sims-like meters and modular ship facilities create divergent player narratives through resource pressure; (2) the "chatter and mood" NPC system, where crew members actively push into player awareness through mood-driven pestering, observable-action-based judgment, and faction-templated dialogue; and (3) the "path-weaving" world design, where procedural worlds gain texture through overlapping NPC arcs and faction missions rather than discrete point-of-interest placement. These three pillars map naturally onto QBN qualities (meter values, mood states, faction standings) that gate storylet availability.

Important caveats: Perko never designed for text-only interfaces, MMO-scale persistence, or trade/economy systems. His visual-spatial design instincts (landmarks, sight lines, density as a spatial property) require significant adaptation for text presentation. His NPC systems assume single-player contexts where the player is the sole audience for NPC behavior. The economic simulation ideas he mentions (resource tiering, inflation control) are explicitly deferred in his own writing and never developed.

## Key Findings

### 1. Survival as "Yarnball": Interlocking Meters That Generate Narrative

Perko's central survival design concept is that survival should not be a binary threat to overcome but a "soft, complicated challenge that interlaces with a lot of other things" ([2019-06-18-space-survival-gameplay.md](qmd://perko/2019-06-18-space-survival-gameplay.md)). Sims-style meters (hunger, filth, loneliness, claustrophobia, boredom) tick upward with each space jump. Ship modules passively reduce meter growth rates, and active facility use provides temporary boosts at resource cost. The player's choice of which modules to install creates divergent narratives without the designer writing them.

- "We didn't write those narratives: we allow the challenges to be faced in a way that turns them into narratives."
- The "dossier" system extends this metaphor to factions: faction rank provides NPC "hardpoints" (diplomats, lawyers, fences) that reduce negative meters like criminality and distrust.
- Different factions impose culturally distinct meter behaviors, creating mechanical differentiation between cultures.

This maps directly to QBN: each meter is a quality, ship module choices gate which storylets fire, and faction-specific meter curves make each faction's narrative space feel different.

### 2. Challenges Must Exert Continual Pressure, Not Be Flattened by Upgrades

Perko criticizes Subnautica's rebreather for eliminating depth pressure entirely: "The challenges in a given mission don't exist to be flattened away, they exist to exert continual pressure" ([2015-05-18-mission-based-iteration-vs-survival-mode.md](qmd://perko/2015-05-18-mission-based-iteration-vs-survival-mode.md)). Environmental constraints should directly shape construction and planning decisions throughout the game, not just in early play.

- Iterative construction (returning to the same challenge with a better design) is the core loop, citing Kerbal Space Program's sandbox mode.
- Construction chains (mining ship to refinery to fleet) create continuity and progression that pure survival games lack ([2015-08-18-good-bad-game-design-pt-2.md](qmd://perko/2015-08-18-good-bad-game-design-pt-2.md)).

### 3. Kill Classes, Use Skill Gradients and Chunky Archetypes

For MMOs specifically, Perko argues that class systems are "an approximation tool to make bookkeeping easier for humans" and should be replaced with continuous skill systems ([2005-12-02-games-with-class.md](qmd://perko/2005-12-02-games-with-class.md)). However, his 2011 Skyrim critique adds nuance: removing classes entirely kills early identity and replayability ([2011-01-13-skyrim.md](qmd://perko/2011-01-13-skyrim.md)).

- Resolution: use chunky starting archetypes tied to the setting (faction, culture, background) that provide immediate identity, then let characters specialize organically through continuous skill use.
- Character creation should double as world introduction: "Instead of carefully weighting their stats, allow players to simply choose A, B, or C -- several times" ([2012-11-06-character-creation-as-world-introduction.md](qmd://perko/2012-11-06-character-creation-as-world-introduction.md)).

### 4. Less Freedom, More Agency -- Choices That Reshape Future Choices

Perko's most consistent CRPG argument: more freedom often means less agency. Binary moral choices are "the plague of RPG dialogue design" because players choose once and then merely reaffirm ([2007-04-09-boolean-choices.md](qmd://perko/2007-04-09-boolean-choices.md), [2009-12-02-an-article-on-choice.md](qmd://perko/2009-12-02-an-article-on-choice.md)).

- Choices must change the avatar's future options, not just set world flags.
- Social/narrative play needs a mechanically complex base system to support it: "It's easier to make social play subordinate to some other game which is complex enough to stand on its own."
- For MMOs: quest results should compile into aggregate world outcomes ("50 players defeat aliens = aliens pushed back") ([2007-05-16-limited-choice-but-big-choices.md](qmd://perko/2007-05-16-limited-choice-but-big-choices.md)).

### 5. Procedural Worlds Need Uniqueness, Coherence, and Gateways

Three requirements for procedural worlds that feel alive ([2010-06-15-complicated.md](qmd://perko/2010-06-15-complicated.md)):

- **Uniqueness stack**: Each location needs distinctive contextual details, not just stat variations. "Ten million locations that vary only statistically won't be nearly as interesting as a hundred locations that are each unique."
- **Coherence ("smearing")**: Uniqueness propagates to neighbors. A gold-rich planet means nearby colonies are wealthier and NPCs discuss the gold.
- **Gateways**: Players need compelling reasons to investigate uniqueness, or it "blends together into a shapeless blah."

The "world weaving" concept ([2016-08-19-world-weaving.md](qmd://perko/2016-08-19-world-weaving.md)) proposes generating worlds from overlapping paths rather than discrete points. NPC arcs, trade routes, and faction agendas are paths; where they intersect, emergent complexity arises. This is architecturally aligned with QBN, where storylets fire at path intersections.

### 6. The Investigate-and-Expand Exploration Pattern

A two-phase pattern for making exploration meaningful ([2015-02-18-investigative-expansive-exploration.md](qmd://perko/2015-02-18-investigative-expansive-exploration.md)):

1. **Investigate**: When players find something interesting, let them zoom in (visit a settlement's districts, talk to people).
2. **Expand**: Once investigated, the location integrates into the player's mission network and generates contextual quests.

- Multiple factions create overlapping missions, intertwining goals.
- Each faction/settlement must be unique enough that players form opinions about them.
- Exploration paired with creation and implicit sharing (player-generated content embedded in others' experiences) is superior to exploration paired with combat-survival ([2014-12-10-exploration-needs-implicit-sharing.md](qmd://perko/2014-12-10-exploration-needs-implicit-sharing.md)).

### 7. The Ship as Home: Psychological Reset and Identity

Every game needs a "home" that serves as a psychological reset ([2008-02-26-home-home-on-the-lagrange.md](qmd://perko/2008-02-26-home-home-on-the-lagrange.md)). Home must be distinct from the main game, customizable, and a hub for all activity. In a merchant ship game, the ship itself is the natural home -- but Perko warns that if the ship IS the game (like the house in The Sims), it loses its restorative function. The ship must feel like a refuge from the dangers outside it.

### 8. NPCs Must Push Into Player Awareness Through Moods and Chatter

Perko's NPC design philosophy is built on two mechanisms:

**Mood system** ([2014-06-09-companions.md](qmd://perko/2014-06-09-companions.md)): NPCs enter medium-to-long-term mood states triggered by situations their personality cares about. Characters in moods pester the player, making themselves visible. Players manage moods by interacting with characters -- spending time, visiting places they enjoy, assigning preferred tasks.

**Chatter system** ([2014-06-11-npc-growth-and-personality.md](qmd://perko/2014-06-11-npc-growth-and-personality.md)): Each NPC statement reveals a concrete in-world state ("The food's getting stale" = supply quality dropping). These statements become reusable interaction tokens the player can reference when giving orders or talking to other NPCs. No natural language parser needed -- chatter provides the verb vocabulary.

- Limit active NPCs to 4--7 to prevent cognitive overload ([2014-03-31-semiautomatic-npcs.md](qmd://perko/2014-03-31-semiautomatic-npcs.md)).
- Use strong "shtick" (2--3 combined archetypes) over statistical variety: "If you have to choose between adding more variation or more shtick options, always go with shtick!"
- Party/crew churn is essential -- send characters on solo missions, settle them at locations, rotate new crew in.

### 9. NPCs Should Judge Observable Actions and Offer Action Plans

NPCs should evaluate the player based on observable behavior, not inferred intentions ([2014-06-18-growing-npcs.md](qmd://perko/2014-06-18-growing-npcs.md)). The Skyrim mod "Arissa" judges thief skills (sneak attacks, lockpicking) rather than ethical quest choices -- and this caused Perko to change his own behavior to impress the NPC, something no other game had achieved.

NPCs should also offer functional action plans, not just respond to dialogue ([2018-09-10-character-driven-game-design.md](qmd://perko/2018-09-10-character-driven-game-design.md)). "Take it out, I'll support you" vs "let's ambush it, follow my lead" are gameplay-embedded plans that advance both the encounter and the NPC relationship.

### 10. Player Identity Emerges from Crew Composition

The player defines their character's personality through companion choice ([2015-08-11-good-bad-game-design.md](qmd://perko/2015-08-11-good-bad-game-design.md)): "Shepard has a character because you mentally justify why these party members are her favorite." This is continuous and ongoing, not a one-time selection. For a merchant ship game, crew hiring choices define the captain's identity: all fighters = pirate captain; skilled traders and navigators = merchant prince.

## Consensus & Disagreement

**Areas of consensus** (recurring themes across all four search angles):

- **Emergent narrative over scripted content** -- supported across survival (yarnball), CRPG (boolean choices critique), world design (path weaving), and NPC design (mood/chatter systems). This is Perko's deepest conviction.
- **Interlocking meters and modular systems** -- ship modules reducing survival meters, NPC moods driven by underlying stats, faction dossier hardpoints, advancement as threshold/spent/acquired combinations. Everything connects.
- **Continual pressure over binary gates** -- survival challenges should not be flattened by upgrades (survival), choices should reshape future options not just set flags (CRPG), exploration should expand into new mission contexts (world), NPCs should create ongoing social pressure through moods (NPC).
- **Fewer, denser content elements over many shallow ones** -- 100 unique locations beat 10,000 statistical ones (world), fewer dense NPCs with relationships beat many thin ones (CRPG/NPC), 4-7 active crew members beat a large cast (NPC).
- **Player identity through constrained choice** -- character creation as world introduction (CRPG), crew composition as personality (NPC), ship module selection as narrative divergence (survival), faction affiliation as cultural identity (world).

**Areas of disagreement**:

- **Classes in CRPGs** -- Perko argues against classes for MMOs in 2005 ("an approximation tool... limiting your MMORPG") but argues for classes in single-player RPGs in 2011 ("removing classes kills replayability"). The tension resolves into "chunky archetypes for initial identity, continuous skills for long-term growth."

**Emerging / uncertain**:

- **Implicit sharing as the successor to combat-survival** in exploration games -- Perko calls this "the most promising replacement" but acknowledges "we don't know how to allow players to create stories. We don't know how to program that tool." This is aspirational, not proven.
- **Economic NPC chatter systems** -- the 2014 post describes NPC statements as economic state indicators that become interaction tokens, but Perko himself notes this was never implemented.

## Gaps & Limitations

- **No trade/economy design**: Perko explicitly defers economic simulation ("resource tiering and keeping inflation under control") and never returns to it in the indexed corpus. This is a critical gap for a merchant ship game.
- **No text-only interface design**: All of Perko's work assumes visual/3D presentation. His emphasis on body language, spatial density, landmarks, and sight lines requires significant adaptation for text. His dialogue template and chatter systems are the most directly transferable.
- **No MMO-scale persistence design**: Only one post touches on MMO frustrations ("persistent world really translates to you can't do shit"). His systems assume a single player as the audience for NPC behavior. Multi-player crew dynamics, shared world economy, and server-scale persistence are unaddressed.
- **No Jack Vance references**: Perko does not reference Vance, the Oikumene, or the baroque cultural differentiation central to Vance's settings. The Vance-specific flavor must come from other sources.
- **Semantic search unavailable**: All searches used BM25 keyword matching only. The vector search embedding model was downloading during the session and never completed. Posts using different terminology for relevant concepts may have been missed.
- **No combat/encounter design coverage**: No dedicated search was run for tactical combat, which may be relevant for piracy and space encounters.
- **Post-2016 coverage thin**: The strongest hits clustered in 2005--2015. Perko's later posts (2016--2019) may contain more refined thinking, but fewer were surfaced.

## Suggested Further Research

- **Trade and economy mechanics**: Perko defers this topic explicitly. Look for other sources on merchant game economies (Patrician series, Sunless Sea/Skies, Traveller RPG trade systems).
- **Text-only adaptation of spatial concepts**: How do density, landmarks, and sight lines translate to text presentation? MUD design literature (Richard Bartle, Raph Koster) may fill this gap.
- **QBN-specific integration**: How do Perko's interlocking meters map to quality-based narrative engines? Failbetter Games' design writing (Alexis Kennedy, Emily Short on StoryNexus) is the natural complement.
- **Multi-player NPC dynamics**: How do NPC mood and chatter systems work when multiple players interact with the same NPCs? This is unaddressed in Perko's single-player-focused writing.
- **Vance-specific cultural mechanics**: How to implement baroque cultural differentiation (etiquette systems, social class, factional custom) in game mechanics. Perko's faction meter curves are a starting point but need Vance-specific elaboration.
- **Re-run searches with semantic search**: The vector search model was unavailable. Re-running with `qmd vsearch` may surface posts using different terminology for relevant concepts.
