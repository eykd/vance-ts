**The Voice of a Baptized Jack Vance**

What would change if Jack Vance were baptized—not merely by cultural contact with Christianity, but by regeneration, by faith, by the deep waters of grace? His pen, of course, would still be Vancian. His sentences would still unspool like silken ropes across a lacquered floor. His fondness for courtly absurdity and exquisite vocabulary would remain. But the world he revealed would be tilted—gently but inexorably—toward the light.

---

### Irony Transfigured

Vance’s irony, in its unbaptized state, is a tool of distance. It protects the author (and the reader) from taking anything too seriously. By treating everything as a performance, he withholds judgment—but also affection. His characters may suffer, but they do so amid theatrical manners and intricate deceits, all under the knowing gaze of a narrator who has seen too much to hope.

But baptize that gaze, and something shifts. Irony does not vanish—but it bends. It no longer serves as insulation from meaninglessness. Instead, it **exposes the false in service of the true**. A baptized Vance might still pen bureaucrats with idiotic protocols or schemers undone by etiquette—but now their unraveling would point to something more than comic symmetry. It would become a judgment—not bitter, but luminous. Irony becomes the surgeon’s scalpel, not the jester’s slapstick.

---

### Beauty as Herald, Not Bauble

Unbaptized, Vance’s beauty is rich, strange, and exquisitely unnecessary. He delights in the curvature of silks, the incantation of invented nouns, the geometry of towers and masks and names. His worlds are museums of dying grandeur.

Baptized, that same aesthetic eye would not be muted—but **reoriented**. Beauty would still be complex, but not decadent; exotic, but not directionless. It would become a **herald of the Real**, an echo of the Logos. Where the unbaptized Vance crafts beauty as spectacle, the baptized Vance reveals beauty as **witness**.

He might still describe a pageant of alien rites—but behind the jeweled masks, something eternal would press outward. His adjectives would remain lush—but now they’d shimmer with longing.

---

### Moral Order—Veiled, but Actual

One of the distinctive features of Vance’s fiction is the moral vacuum his characters often inhabit. Customs vary wildly between cultures, and so morality becomes performative, local, and brittle. In such a world, cleverness is survival, and virtue is usually impractical.

But imagine that same apparatus infused with the **moral gravity of grace**. The baptized Vance would still permit absurd laws, corrupt guilds, and farcical rites—but **beneath them would stir a quiet order**, rooted not in fashion but in truth. His protagonists might still be rogues and wanderers, but they would now be capable of repentance. He would allow not only the triumph of wit, but the intrusion of conscience.

Some characters might still scoff at holiness—but others would glimpse it, perhaps through an orphan’s hymn, a dying man’s gesture, or the glint of undeserved mercy. The moral field would still be dry, cracked, and strange—but the **seed of righteousness** would be planted.

---

### Hope Without Sentiment

Perhaps the most profound difference would be the presence of **hope**. Not sentimentality. Not tidy endings. But the kind of hope that knows the stars are ancient, the world is weary, and still **redemption is possible**.

Unbaptized, Vance’s endings often dwell in ironic futility: the con man outwits the tyrant, only to be conned in turn; the hero survives, but the world remains absurd. Meaning is elusive, if not denied.

Baptized, Vance might still end on a quiet note—but now it would resonate. There would be **echoes of the eschaton**, hints of the new heavens behind the dry leaves of the old. The tale might still close with a cynical smile—but behind the smile, **a scarred but shining joy**.

---

### A Final Note

To baptize Jack Vance is not to strip him of his irony, his elegance, or his crystalline prose. It is to introduce **a center that holds**—a gravity deeper than decorum, a joy more enduring than wit, a beauty whose source is not invention, but glory.

Such a voice would still entertain. But it would also ache. And through its artifice, a reader might find not just amusement, but mercy.
