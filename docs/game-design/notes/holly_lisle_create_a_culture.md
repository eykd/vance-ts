# Holly Lisle's Create A Culture: A Comprehensive Guide & Master Checklist

_Based on Holly Lisle's "Create A Culture Clinic" (The Worldbuilding Course: Book II)_

---

## How to Use This Guide

This guide follows Holly Lisle's four-section framework: **Foundation**, **Basic Culture Building** (Home Life, Community, Religion, Government), **Advanced Techniques**, and **Organization**. Use it as a working reference — you do not need to answer every question. Pick the questions that matter for your story, answer them as broadly or as deeply as you need, and stop when you know enough to write.

Holly Lisle's core principle: **Build as you go. Only build what you need. Start writing before you've finished worldbuilding.**

---

## Part One: Foundation

### What Is a Culture?

Every real, functional culture — whether in the real world or invented — shares all seven of the following characteristics. Use this as your baseline test.

#### ✅ The Seven Characteristics Checklist

- [ ] Members share **common ground** (a shared context, activity, or identity)
- [ ] They are born of a **shared philosophy** (a core belief or worldview)
- [ ] They adhere to **specific goals** (not just opposition to something else)
- [ ] Which require the **setting aside of differences** (unity over faction)
- [ ] And demand **personal sacrifices** of time, effort, and resources
- [ ] In order for all members to **work for the good of the group**
- [ ] And to **survive, propagate, and grow** beyond the lifetimes of current members

> **Warning:** A group that only defines itself by what it _opposes_ — without offering concrete values, goals, or alternatives — is a **rabble**, not a culture. Rabbles are short-lived. Countercultures and subcultures must still meet all seven criteria; they simply do so in opposition to or variation from the main culture.

---

### Subcultures and Countercultures

#### ✅ Subculture Checklist

- [ ] Shares all basic tenets of the main culture
- [ ] Adds additional goals, sacrifices, or philosophical requirements
- [ ] Has **narrower** common ground (more exclusive, more restrictive)
- [ ] Propagates by cherry-picking from the main culture's membership

#### ✅ Counterculture Checklist

- [ ] Contains each of the seven characteristics in fully-formed fashion
- [ ] Offers a **complete philosophy of its own** (not just "we reject X")
- [ ] Provides **creative, not merely destructive**, goals for its members
- [ ] Defines what it _is_, not only what it _isn't_
- [ ] Propagates by recruiting those dissatisfied with the main culture

---

### Function vs. Dysfunction

A **functional culture** works. That's why it survives. A dysfunctional culture is a dead culture. If you want to write dysfunctional groups, know what the _functional_ baseline looks like first — the dysfunction is only legible against the norm.

Members of dysfunctional groups within a culture will:

- Lose or destroy their common ground
- Have "anti" goals (against things) without being able to say what they are _for_
- Emphasize personal differences over commonality
- Refuse personal sacrifices of time, effort, and resources
- Consider only their own good, not the group's
- Fight against the propagation and survival of their culture

> Build your culture's functional baseline first. Dysfunction is variation from — and only comprehensible in relation to — the norm.

---

### Avoiding Thinness

The biggest problem with invented culture is **thinness** — a world that feels like a painted backdrop with nothing behind it. Signs of thinness:

- Every character is the same age, class, or profession
- Nobody knows how to cook, farm, build, heal, or do the unglamorous things that keep a society running
- The only people present are those directly relevant to the plot
- Background characters feel like props

**The cure:** Build community breadth. Reference characters who exist because the _world_ needs them, even if the story doesn't — the plowshare-maker, the dyer, the midwife, the tax collector.

## Part Two: Basic Culture Building

Basic culture building is divided into four sections: **Home Life**, **Community**, **Religion**, and **Government**. For each section, Holly Lisle recommends:

1. Read all the relevant questions
2. Mark the ones that interest you or directly relate to your story
3. Answer in any order
4. Decide whether to go **broad** (answer many questions lightly) or **deep** (use Advanced Techniques on one question)
5. Stop when you know enough to write the next scene

---

## Section A: Home Life

Home life is the most personal layer of culture. It covers how individuals live — as singles, as pairs, and as families — and how they move through the stages of life from birth to death.

---

### A1: Singles

Singleness carries wildly different meaning across cultures — sacred vocation in one, mark of failure in another, neutral choice in a third.

#### ✅ Single Life Checklist

**Defining singleness:**

- [ ] What are the methods by which a person becomes single? (choice, unchosen in a marriage market, widowed, set aside as sacred, etc.)
- [ ] Is singleness chosen by the individual, or by others?
- [ ] Is singleness-by-death-of-partner viewed differently from original-state singleness?
- [ ] How is singleness-by-choice viewed?
- [ ] How is involuntary singleness viewed?

**Reasons for singleness:**

- [ ] What wonderful reasons for singleness exist in this culture?
- [ ] What acceptable reasons exist?
- [ ] What unacceptable reasons exist?
- [ ] Are there specific barriers that force singleness (limited marriage pools, gender imbalances, caste restrictions, inability to afford a dowry)?

**Social identity of singles:**

- [ ] What social traditions are observed exclusively by single people?
- [ ] Are there specific holidays for singles?
- [ ] Specific foods, dietary requirements, or cooking techniques that mark the single state?
- [ ] Specific garments, hairstyles, jewelry, or skin markings?
- [ ] Do singles have specific daily rituals? Are they public or private? Secular or religious?

**Singles in relation to others:**

- [ ] Do singles maintain a place within their families of origin?
- [ ] Do they have a specific connection to other singles?
- [ ] What is their role in relation to pairs and families?
- [ ] Does singleness affect social status or caste? How?
- [ ] Does gender change any of these relationships?

**Work and sexuality:**

- [ ] What jobs or callings are banned for single persons?
- [ ] What jobs or callings exist specifically for single persons?
- [ ] Do singles receive preferred, equal, or substandard treatment in workplaces?
- [ ] What taboos govern the sexuality of singles?
- [ ] What special sexual freedoms exist for singles?
- [ ] Does gender affect what is forbidden and what is permitted?

**Cultural ideal and conflict:**

- [ ] Write out: _"The perfect single [male/female/person] in this culture will appear to those around them as..."_
- [ ] Where are singles splitting along conformist/rebel lines?
- [ ] What defines success or failure for singles in this culture?
- [ ] How does a single person go about changing life role to paired or familied?

---

### A2: Pairs

Pairing structures are among the most variable aspects of any culture. Do not default to your own culture's expectations.

#### ✅ Paired Life Checklist

**Becoming a pair:**

- [ ] What are the methods by which a person becomes a member of a pair? (self-selection, parental arrangement, lottery, lottery by a priest, fights to the death, spirit quest, negotiation, etc.)
- [ ] Is the pairing chosen by the individual or by others?
- [ ] Are self-chosen pairings viewed differently from arranged pairings?

**Reasons for pairing:**

- [ ] What wonderful reasons for pairing exist?
- [ ] What acceptable reasons exist?
- [ ] What unacceptable reasons exist?
- [ ] Is pairing mandatory, optional, or dependent on status?

**Social identity of pairs:**

- [ ] What social traditions are observed exclusively by paired-but-not-familied people?
- [ ] Are there specific holidays, foods, garments, hairstyles, jewelry, or markings for pairs?
- [ ] Do pairs have specific daily rituals? Private or public? Secular or religious?

**Pairs in relation to others:**

- [ ] Do pairs maintain a place within their families of origin?
- [ ] Do they have a specific connection to other pairs?
- [ ] What is their role in relation to singles and families?
- [ ] Does being paired affect status or caste? How?
- [ ] Does gender change any of these relationships?

**Work and sexuality:**

- [ ] What jobs or callings are banned for paired-but-not-familied persons?
- [ ] What jobs or callings exist specifically for paired persons?
- [ ] Do paired persons receive preferred, equal, or substandard treatment in workplaces?
- [ ] What are the specific expectations toward childbearing?
- [ ] What taboos govern the sexuality of paired persons?
- [ ] What special freedoms exist?
- [ ] Does gender affect what is forbidden and what is permitted?
- [ ] How do pairs interact with singles and families in matters of sexuality?

**Cultural ideal and conflict:**

- [ ] Write out: _"The perfect paired [male/female/person] in this culture will appear to those around them as..."_
- [ ] Where are pairs splitting along conformist/rebel lines?
- [ ] What defines success or failure for pairs?

---

### A3: Families

Family structures are among the most contested and variable aspects of culture. Avoid defaulting to nuclear family assumptions.

#### ✅ Family Life Checklist

**Becoming a family:**

- [ ] What are the methods by which a person becomes part of a family? (marriage, cohabitation, adoption, invitation, birth)
- [ ] Must the family come from a previously recognized pairing, or can it be legitimate from other sources?
- [ ] Are there methods by which families can be approved or disapproved by the culture?

**Reasons for family formation:**

- [ ] What wonderful reasons for forming families exist?
- [ ] What acceptable reasons exist?
- [ ] What unacceptable reasons exist?
- [ ] Is family formation mandatory?

**Family structures to consider:**

- [ ] Nuclear (one adult pair + children)?
- [ ] Extended multigenerational household?
- [ ] Multiple spouses (polygyny, polyandry)?
- [ ] Communal family structures?
- [ ] State-controlled reproductive pools?
- [ ] Child-gender preferences or imbalances? (How do these affect family life?)
- [ ] Dowry systems? What do they cost? Who suffers under them?

**Social identity of families:**

- [ ] What social traditions are observed exclusively by familied people?
- [ ] Are there specific holidays, foods, garments, hairstyles, jewelry, or markings?
- [ ] Do families have specific daily rituals? Private or public? Secular or religious?

**Families in relation to others:**

- [ ] Do families maintain a place within their families of origin?
- [ ] Do they have a specific connection to other families?
- [ ] What is their role in relation to singles and pairs?
- [ ] Does being part of a family affect status or caste?
- [ ] Does gender affect any of these relationships?

**Work and sexuality:**

- [ ] What jobs or callings are banned for familied persons?
- [ ] What jobs or callings exist specifically for familied persons?
- [ ] Do familied persons receive preferred, equal, or substandard treatment?
- [ ] What are the specific expectations toward childbearing?
- [ ] What taboos govern sexuality within families?
- [ ] What special freedoms exist?
- [ ] How does the age of family members affect what is forbidden and permitted?

**Cultural ideal and conflict:**

- [ ] Write out: _"The perfect family in this culture will appear to those around it as..."_
- [ ] Write out the ideal for adult males, adult females, and children separately
- [ ] Where are families splitting along conformist/rebel lines?
- [ ] What defines success or failure for a family?

---

### A4: The Life Cycle

Every character in your story exists somewhere on a continuum from birth to death. The richness of a world comes from including people at every stage.

#### ✅ Childbirth Checklist

**Social traditions:**

- [ ] What preparations do parents make before birth? Any taboos or required foods, colors, or clothing?
- [ ] What restrictions are placed on the mother during pregnancy? On the father?
- [ ] Is the birth surrounded by fear or joy? Are evil spirits warded off, or good spirits welcomed?
- [ ] Is the process treated as medical or natural? Are families present or kept away?
- [ ] How is the newborn welcomed when circumstances are favorable?
- [ ] How is the newborn made unwelcome when circumstances are unfavorable?
- [ ] Does gender of the child matter to welcome or rejection?
- [ ] Does health matter? Parentage? Social status of the family? Marital status?
- [ ] Does the culture test newborns for fitness to survive?
- [ ] Are there circumstances in which a newborn might be marked for death?

**Rituals:**

- [ ] What family obligations exist before, during, or after birth?
- [ ] What community obligations exist?
- [ ] What religious obligations exist?
- [ ] What government obligations exist? (birth registration, naming laws, etc.)

**Expectations and success:**

- [ ] What does the family expect from the birth?
- [ ] Does the time or date of birth figure into expectations?
- [ ] What counts as a successful birth in this culture?
- [ ] What counts as a failed birth?
- [ ] Are there non-obvious criteria (gender, birthmark, lucky/unlucky position in birth order)?

---

#### ✅ Childhood Checklist

**Social traditions:**

- [ ] Before they can walk and talk, how are children treated and tended? By whom?
- [ ] How does this change once they can walk and talk?
- [ ] How does it change once they can reason (around age six)?
- [ ] At what age are they considered useful to the household or economy?
- [ ] How are these stages marked? Special clothes? Hairstyles?

**Rituals:**

- [ ] Does the culture celebrate rituals marking the passing stages of childhood?

**Expectations:**

- [ ] What do parents expect of a child at each stage?
- [ ] Do boys and girls have different expected roles?
- [ ] Are expectations different for the firstborn vs. the lastborn?
- [ ] How do expectations vary across social strata?
- [ ] How do the parents' occupations affect expectations on children?

**Education and value:**

- [ ] How is education carried out? Apprenticeship? Home learning? Fostering? Mentoring? Classroom?
- [ ] What careers are possible, and how do children prepare for them?
- [ ] Are careers chosen by status, ability, competition, lottery, gender, race, or oracle?
- [ ] What barriers prevent some children from obtaining the education others receive? (Slavery? Caste? Poverty? Illegitimacy? Ugliness? Race? Gender?)

**Curiosity and conformity:**

- [ ] How does this culture treat the question "Why?" from a child?
- [ ] How does it treat persistently curious children?
- [ ] What defines the perfect child of this culture?
- [ ] What defines the failed child?

---

#### ✅ Young Adulthood Checklist

**Social traditions:**

- [ ] How does the culture determine who is and is not a young adult? (Age? Achievement? Coming-of-age test? Religious passage? Elder approval?)
- [ ] What part do the young adult's own actions play in achieving this status?
- [ ] Is adulthood seen as an achievement, or do people glorify the helplessness of childhood?
- [ ] What privileges does young adulthood confer?
- [ ] Are some privileges withheld for a later stage?

**Rituals:**

- [ ] What family rituals mark the entrance into young adulthood?
- [ ] What community rituals? (Hazing? Presentation to society? A party?)
- [ ] What religious rituals? (Coming-of-age ceremony? Assumption of a role?)
- [ ] What government rituals? (Fealty? Military service? Right to vote? Obligation of taxes?)

**Work:**

- [ ] Is the young adult a fully functioning member of society, or still considered untried?
- [ ] How does gender, race, or status change expectations?
- [ ] What work is open to the young adult that is closed to children or full adults?
- [ ] Can the young adult change jobs, choose their path, pursue dreams?

**Sexuality:**

- [ ] How does the young adult find a mate? Is there choice in the process?
- [ ] Is immediate family formation expected, or is a period of singleness permissible?
- [ ] If singleness is permitted, what expressions of sexuality are allowed?
- [ ] What happens to young adults who stray outside the permissible?

**Rebellion:**

- [ ] How does the culture view rebellion from young adults?
- [ ] How does it treat the rebellious young adult?
- [ ] How does a young adult rebel and still survive?

**Success/failure:**

- [ ] Write: _"The perfect young adult [male/female] in this culture will..."_
- [ ] Write: _"The failed young adult [male/female] in this culture will..."_

---

#### ✅ Full Adulthood Checklist

**Social traditions:**

- [ ] How does the culture determine who is and is not a full adult?
- [ ] Are there tests, rituals, mandatory service, or other requirements?
- [ ] Are there gender, race, or caste barriers to full adulthood?
- [ ] What privileges does full adulthood confer? Are there multiple layers?
- [ ] What privileges are withheld for even later stages?

**Rituals:**

- [ ] What family rituals mark entrance into full adulthood? (First child? Opening a business? Place in family hierarchy?)
- [ ] What community rituals? (Secret society admission? Taking on apprentices?)
- [ ] What religious rituals? (Elder role? Teaching role? Leadership?)
- [ ] What government rituals? (Military completion? Right to collect taxes? Right to bestow rank?)

**Work:**

- [ ] Is the full adult expected to innovate, or to guard tradition?
- [ ] What work is open to full adults that isn't open to others?
- [ ] What work is closed to them that is open to others?
- [ ] Is career change possible? Can dreams still be pursued?

**Sexuality:**

- [ ] How does the full adult find or keep a mate? How do they replace a lost mate?
- [ ] Does the culture press the full adult to expand family, accumulate spouses, or support children's households?
- [ ] Is sexual experimentation permitted?

**Rebellion:**

- [ ] How does the culture view rebellion from full adults?
- [ ] What makes full adult rebellion more risky than young adult rebellion?
- [ ] How does a full adult rebel and still survive?

**Success/failure:**

- [ ] Write: _"The perfect full adult [male/female] in this culture will..."_
- [ ] Write: _"The failed full adult [male/female] in this culture will..."_

---

#### ✅ Old Age Checklist

**Social traditions:**

- [ ] How does the culture determine who is and is not an elder?
- [ ] Is old age seen as an achievement, or just something that happens?
- [ ] Do people try to fend it off? How?
- [ ] What privileges does elder status confer? Are there multiple layers?

**Rituals:**

- [ ] What family rituals mark entrance into old age? (First grandchild? Last child wed? Retirement from work?)
- [ ] What community rituals? (Leadership positions? Master-craftsman status?)
- [ ] What religious rituals? (Handing off responsibilities? Overseeing new leaders?)
- [ ] What government rituals? (Retirement from military service? Release from taxes? Right to bestow property or rank?)

**Work:**

- [ ] Is the elder expected to teach, transmit heritage, or create wisdom?
- [ ] What work is open to elders that is closed to others?
- [ ] What work is closed to elders that is open to others?
- [ ] Does the elder look forward to security, or into poverty and darkness?

**Sexuality:**

- [ ] How does the elder deal with a lost mate? Can they find a new one?
- [ ] What responsibilities do elders have toward their adult progeny?
- [ ] Is sexual expression still permitted for elders?

**The cost of aging:**

- [ ] Does this culture fight to prolong life? In which cases?
- [ ] How do families, community, religion, and government care for dying elders?
- [ ] What respect does the culture show its dying members?

**Success/failure:**

- [ ] Write: _"The perfect elder [male/female] in this culture will..."_
- [ ] Write: _"The failed elder [male/female] in this culture will..."_

---

#### ✅ Death Checklist

**Social traditions:**

- [ ] How does the culture treat the death of one of its members?
- [ ] Is treatment different by age, status, gender, or relationship type?
- [ ] Is treatment different by cause of death? (Suicide vs. murder, disease, accident, old age)
- [ ] How does the family deal with death and grief?
- [ ] How does the community deal with death and grief?
- [ ] How does religion deal with death and grief?
- [ ] How does government deal with death and grief?

**Rituals:**

- [ ] What rituals do family and friends perform for the dead?
- [ ] What rituals does the community perform or require from the bereaved?
- [ ] What rituals does religion perform or require?
- [ ] What rituals does government perform or require? (Mandatory mourning? Taxes? Confiscation of property? Inheritance laws?)

**Expectations:**

- [ ] How does the culture view the afterlife of the dead?
- [ ] How does that future vary with how the person lived?
- [ ] How does it vary with status, gender, age, or religious/government affiliation?

**Success/failure in death:**

- [ ] How does this culture define a successful death?
- [ ] How does it define a failed death?
- [ ] What variables (religion, status, gender, age, cause of death) change the verdict?

### A5: Personal Housing

#### ✅ Housing Checklist

- [ ] What housing is available to singles? In which neighborhoods? How does this vary by age, status, gender, race?
- [ ] What housing is available to pairs?
- [ ] What housing is available to families?
- [ ] Who builds homes? Does this vary by status, gender, religion, race?
- [ ] Is there a community component to home building? (Barn-raising equivalents?)
- [ ] Is there a religious component? (Blessings, spells, sacrifice, orientation, consecrated ground?)
- [ ] Is there a government component? (Forms, permits, bribes, land ownership rules?)
- [ ] What rituals do new home inhabitants go through when moving in?

## Section B: Community

Community is where private life meets public life. It is all the people a culture needs to survive and keep going.

> **Remember:** Rich communities include people at every level of every occupation, even those the plot doesn't directly need. The breadth of a community — its farmers and bakers and rope-makers and midwives and accountants — is what makes a world feel real.

---

### B1: Philosophy in Action

#### ✅ Philosophy vs. Actions Checklist

- [ ] What does your community _claim_ to stand for? What groups does it claim to support? What rights does it claim to uphold?
- [ ] How do its actions prove or give lie to those words?
- [ ] Is the community monocultural, or does it contain multiple cultures with competing needs?
- [ ] How does it treat each of the cultures within it?

---

### B2: Social Groups

#### ✅ Friendships Checklist

- [ ] In which situations does the culture accept friendships? Does age, gender, social status, or race matter?
- [ ] In which situations does it forbid friendships?
- [ ] How do friends in this culture meet, acknowledge each other, and benefit from friendship?
- [ ] Do friends bear specific titles, use special language, or have special terms of address?
- [ ] Do friendships have taboo discussion topics that would destroy them if broached?
- [ ] Do friendships have taboo actions?
- [ ] Does the culture support different kinds of friendship? What are they? What do they share?

#### ✅ Gender Relationships Checklist

- [ ] How does the culture view non-sexual relationships outside the family?
- [ ] How do non-family, non-friend community members relate to each other?
- [ ] Can members of different genders spend time together in purely social situations? In what circumstances? With what oversight?
- [ ] How do age, race, religion, and other factors shape these relationships?

#### ✅ Work Relationships Checklist

- [ ] How do work relationships in this culture reinforce cultural values?
- [ ] What sorts of work most fit the culture's ideals?
- [ ] What sorts of work most challenge or undermine the culture's ideals?
- [ ] How does the culture deal with work that encourages rebellion?

#### ✅ Status Relationships Checklist

- [ ] What classes or layers of social strata exist? Who will associate with whom?
- [ ] What are the compelling needs of each class?
- [ ] Do language barriers exist for moving up or down in status?
- [ ] Do social barriers exist?
- [ ] Do legal barriers exist?
- [ ] Do gender barriers exist?
- [ ] Do race barriers exist?
- [ ] Do religious barriers exist?
- [ ] How do these barriers affect cross-status relationships?

#### ✅ Play, Sport, and Challenge Checklist

- [ ] What sports or games are favored in this culture?
- [ ] How are these games played, and how are players chosen?
- [ ] Which favor individual achievement? Which favor team achievement?
- [ ] When does the culture begin training people into various sports?
- [ ] Does the community have a stake in winning or losing beyond pride? (Gambling? Lives? Territory?)
- [ ] Are some sports accessible only to certain classes, genders, or races?
- [ ] What rides on winning or losing in each sport?

---

### B3: War and Peace

#### ✅ War and Peace Checklist

Review the spectrum: pure pacifism → active defense → passive offense → active offense → agressor. Where does your culture fall?

- [ ] Does your culture believe anything is worth fighting to protect?
- [ ] Under what specific circumstances will they attack first?
- [ ] Under what circumstances will they defend others?
- [ ] Under what circumstances will they defend themselves?
- [ ] What are the conditions they place on peace?

---

### B4: Civic Structures

#### ✅ Civic Buildings Checklist

**Business structures:**

- [ ] What sorts of buildings do businesses construct? Where are they located?
- [ ] How do they vary by usage, owner status, or anticipated clientele?
- [ ] Who benefits from these businesses? Who suffers?

**Community structures:**

- [ ] What civic gathering spaces exist? (Parks, arenas, theaters, public squares)
- [ ] Who is permitted to use them? How are they maintained?

**Religious structures:**

- [ ] What buildings does religion construct? Where?
- [ ] How do they vary by religious status and wealth?
- [ ] What activities take place there? Are any destructive or secret?
- [ ] Are worshippers aware of the full range of activities?

**Government structures:**

- [ ] What buildings does government construct? Where?
- [ ] How do they vary by government wealth and community standing?
- [ ] What activities take place there? Who benefits? Who suffers?

**Building process:**

- [ ] Who builds civic structures?
- [ ] Is there a community participation component?
- [ ] Is there a religious component? (Blessings? Consecration? Required orientation?)
- [ ] Is there a government component? (Forms, permits, bribes, ownership rules?)
- [ ] What rituals do new civic building users go through?

---

### B5: Community Divisions

#### ✅ Community Groups Checklist

- [ ] What community groups exist in your culture? (Religious, civic, sporting, secret, political)
- [ ] What are each group's goals?
- [ ] Who are their members?
- [ ] How do members of unrelated groups view each other?
- [ ] What are the _acceptable_ divisions — those most people support? Why?
- [ ] What are the _questionable_ divisions — those that make about a third of non-members suspicious? Why?
- [ ] What are the _unacceptable_ divisions? What do they stand for? Who supports them and why?

---

### B6: Cohesiveness vs. Diversity

#### ✅ Cultural Cohesion Checklist

- [ ] How does your culture balance cohesiveness (common ground) with diversity (openness to new ideas)?
- [ ] Does it have a saying like "The nail that sticks up will be hammered down," or more "An it harm none, do as you will," or something between?
- [ ] What rabbles has it developed? How has it dealt with them?
- [ ] Has the culture grown more open or more closed over time?
- [ ] How well is it surviving in relation to its neighbors?

---

### B7: Money and Commerce

#### ✅ Economy Checklist

Identify your economy type: Barter → Feudal/Coercive → Communist/Allocative → Capitalist → Mixed. Consider that black markets arise wherever desired goods are suppressed.

- [ ] How does your community determine the value of goods and labor?
- [ ] What sorts of things can be owned? (Land? Gold? People?)
- [ ] Can individuals own businesses and property, or does the government own all?
- [ ] Where are the markets? Who may attend them?
- [ ] Who oversees tax collection?

**Production:**

- [ ] What foods does this culture grow in surplus?
- [ ] What foods must it trade for?
- [ ] What household items does it produce? (Cloth, clothing, cookware, tools, soap, etc.)
- [ ] What household items must it import?
- [ ] What technological or craft items does it produce? (Pottery, weapons, armor, ships, glass, etc.)
- [ ] What must it import?
- [ ] With whom does it trade? What cultural conflicts arise from these trading relationships?
- [ ] How is production determined — by free market, by government allocation, by feudal mandate?

**Distribution:**

- [ ] Who are the _haves_? How did they get their surplus?
- [ ] Who are the _have-nots_? Why?
- [ ] Who gets to consume the goods produced, and what must they do to obtain them?

## Section C: Religion

Religion is perhaps the richest vein in culture-building. What a people worship tells you nearly everything about them.

> **Holly Lisle's core insight:** Religions are not all the same. They vary enormously in scope (how much power they have over individual life), content (what they believe), philosophy (why they believe it), and format (how they practice it). Build religions as carefully as characters — they have histories, agendas, internal tensions, and the capacity to change from good to bad and back.

---

### C1: Philosophy of Religion

#### ✅ Religious Philosophy Checklist

- [ ] What is the one-line statement that sums up this religion's core truth? (Its tag line)
- [ ] How does the religion explain the universe? What is the nature of the divine?
- [ ] How does it categorize the relationship between humans and the divine?
- [ ] How does it categorize the relationship between humans and each other?
- [ ] How does it view life and death? What value does the individual human have?
- [ ] How does it define morality? Good and evil? Faith? Tolerance? Justice?
- [ ] How does it view the relationship between religion and the individual? Between religion and community? Between religion and government?
- [ ] How do believers benefit by following this religion?
- [ ] How do they suffer by following it?
- [ ] How does it make their community ties stronger — or does it separate them from community?

**Choose a theological category as a starting point:**

- [ ] Animism (everything is alive and connected)
- [ ] Monotheism (one god, possibly many saints/angels/demons)
- [ ] Polytheism (one religion, many gods)
- [ ] Dualism (two forces in opposition)
- [ ] Deism (supreme being revealed through reason and observation)
- [ ] Naturalism (supernatural and natural are the same)
- [ ] Humanism (value of humanity without the supernatural)
- [ ] Nihilism (life has no inherent meaning)
- [ ] Atheism (no god exists)
- [ ] Other (see the full taxonomy in Holly Lisle's book)

---

### C2: Scope of Religion

Scope determines how much power the religion can actually exercise.

#### ✅ Religious Scope Checklist

- [ ] Does the religion control public education? Private education? Does it forbid other books?
- [ ] Does it attack other religions, require avoidance of non-members, or forbid other religions from existing?
- [ ] Does it have the power to enforce that demand?
- [ ] Do its mandates require specific moral actions? Does it enforce them through shame, dialogue, or legal control?
- [ ] Does it control the law for its own members? For everyone?
- [ ] Can it influence political leaders through dialogue? Through mass action? Through force?
- [ ] Does it have its own army?
- [ ] Does it reach into private family life? Can it demand members monitor each other and report transgressions?
- [ ] Can a member be killed for leaving?

---

### C3: Outreach and Membership

#### ✅ Religious Membership Checklist

- [ ] How does this religion gain new members? (Birth? Voluntary conversion? Conquest? Proselytism?)
- [ ] Are members required to seek converts? Which members? What tools are they given?
- [ ] Does the religion permit members to quit? Under what circumstances?
- [ ] What specific steps does quitting require?
- [ ] Does the religion penalize those who quit? How? (Shunning? Excommunication? Death?)
- [ ] How does it treat the families of those who quit?

---

### C4: Content of Religion

#### ✅ Religious Content Checklist

- [ ] What do believers believe? How is it categorized?
- [ ] Where do their beliefs originate? (Splinter from an existing faith? Evolution of a prior religion? New invention by a visionary? Government creation?)
- [ ] What actions do their beliefs encourage?
  - [ ] Do they seek to make the world better? How?
  - [ ] Do they seek to make followers' lives better? How?
  - [ ] Do they seek to benefit non-believers? How?
- [ ] What actions do their beliefs permit or require that make the world _worse_?
  - [ ] How do they make followers' lives worse?
  - [ ] How do they harm non-believers?
  - [ ] Are these harmful actions supported or required by the religion's stated philosophy?

---

### C5: Format of Worship

#### ✅ Gods, Saints, and Supernatural Beings Checklist

- [ ] Who are this religion's gods? What do they stand for? Do they have a hierarchy?
- [ ] Do the gods have a past history? A future destiny?
- [ ] How do the gods feel about their worshippers? How do worshippers feel about them?
- [ ] What do the gods require of worshippers? How do they enforce those requirements?
- [ ] What do worshippers require of the gods? How do they ask? How reliable are the gods at delivering?
- [ ] Does the religion have saints, prophets, angels, demons, spirits, or deified ancestors? What roles do they play?

#### ✅ Ceremonies and Services Checklist

- [ ] Does the religion have regular services? If so, what is the order of service?
- [ ] What do priests/ministers do? What do the people do? What do the gods do?
- [ ] Are there situational ceremonies? (Birth, death, marriage, divorce, change of seasons, change of leadership)
- [ ] Do genders worship differently? Age groups? Races?

#### ✅ Holy Days and Holidays Checklist

- [ ] What regular holidays does the religion celebrate?
- [ ] Are any tied to planetary or astronomical positions?
- [ ] Are any tied to seasons?
- [ ] Are any tied to the birth or death cycles of gods?
- [ ] Are any tied to human historical or allegorical events? (The day humans were expelled from paradise; the day a human married a god)
- [ ] How is each holiday celebrated? Is it joyful, solemn, terrifying?
- [ ] Are any holidays actually dangerous? Who might not survive them?

#### ✅ Private Worship Checklist

- [ ] Does this religion have a private component? What elements are practiced alone at home?
- [ ] Are there private rituals or liturgies?
- [ ] Does a private person speak with the gods differently than an official priest?
- [ ] Does private communication have the religion's sanction, or is it a rogue element?
- [ ] Is private worship persecuted?

#### ✅ Religious Buildings Checklist

- [ ] Does the religion use specific buildings? Public or private?
- [ ] Do buildings serve purposes beyond worship? (Libraries, schools, childcare, elder care, charity)
- [ ] Are buildings ever used for destructive purposes? (Imprisonment, execution, human sacrifice, Star Chamber meetings)
- [ ] Are worshippers aware of all activities that occur in religious buildings?

---

## Section D: Government

Government is, in Holly Lisle's words, where the rock meets the hard place. It is almost universally corrupt to some degree. The interesting question is not whether corruption exists, but what form it takes and who suffers.

> **Key insight:** Government creates its own form of common ground — usually by creating a common ground for favored cultures and a separate, inferior common ground for despised ones. This asymmetry is the source of enormous fictional conflict.

---

### D1: Government and Culture

#### ✅ Government–Culture Relationship Checklist

- [ ] How does the government relate to the main culture? How does it relate to other cultures it governs?
- [ ] How does the culture in power treat the cultures it governs?
- [ ] How do non-dominant cultures work with the government?
- [ ] How do they work against it?

---

### D2: The Hierarchy of Power

#### ✅ Executive Power Checklist

- [ ] Who is the recognized head of government? (Hereditary monarch? Elected president? Council? High Priest? Warlord? Biggest brain?)
- [ ] Is the executive office a figurehead, or does it hold real power?
- [ ] How does the leader delegate power?
- [ ] To whom does the leader willingly listen? Why?
- [ ] To whom does the leader unwillingly listen? Why?
- [ ] If the leader is a figurehead, who holds real power? How did they get it? How do they keep it?
- [ ] Who opposes the leader? Why?
- [ ] How deep does the leader's power go? Can they reach into individual homes? Are they above the law?

#### ✅ Legislative Power Checklist

- [ ] Who makes the laws? How did they get that authority?
- [ ] How do they handle old, outdated laws?
- [ ] Is there a guiding document they must follow? (A constitution, religious text, historic epic)
- [ ] Are there subjects about which they may not make laws?
- [ ] Are all people subject to the same laws, or do separate laws apply to separate groups?

#### ✅ Judicial Power Checklist

- [ ] What sort of justice system exists? Who judges the accused?
- [ ] Is innocence presumed until guilt is proven, or is the accused presumed guilty?
- [ ] Are there checks and balances? A mandatory representative for the accused? A jury?
- [ ] Are sentences determined by a fixed code, or at the judge's whim?
- [ ] Who carries out sentences? (Prison? Work farms? Slavery? Execution?)
- [ ] Can a criminal's family be punished for the criminal's actions?

#### ✅ Military Power Checklist

- [ ] What sort of military does this culture have?
- [ ] To what or whom does it swear allegiance? (A nation? A constitution? A person?)
- [ ] Does it have a history of coup attempts or successful coups?
- [ ] Is it a permanent standing force or a contingency force?
- [ ] What training does it receive?
- [ ] What hierarchy does it have?
- [ ] Is military membership voluntary or involuntary?
- [ ] What benefits come with membership? (Citizenship? Healthcare? Housing? Regular meals?)
- [ ] Are the families of members cared for?
- [ ] Does the surrounding population approve of the military?
- [ ] Does the military have clear external threats to address?
- [ ] Does the military have a positive peacetime role?

#### ✅ Surreptitious Power Checklist

- [ ] What hidden power-holders exist? Secret societies? Corporate interests? Religious groups with invisible political connections?
- [ ] How do they work? Who can be members?
- [ ] Are they government insiders, or outside manipulators?
- [ ] What are their goals?
- [ ] Are they basically good — working against corruption?
- [ ] Are they basically evil — acquiring power at others' expense?
- [ ] Does the official leader take bribes or bend to their pressure?

---

## Part Three: Advanced Techniques

Advanced techniques take you _deep_ into one narrow area. They are optional, but they transform a broad world into a lived-in one. Use them when a particular element is critical to your story.

**Holly Lisle's Rules for Advanced Techniques:**

1. **List before building.** Often, knowing that cities have libraries and bakeries' guilds is enough. You may not need to map them.
2. **Only build what you need.** If you need the stable layout, you don't also need the granary, the coop, and the pig wallow.
3. **Build as you go.** Don't pre-build everything you _think_ you'll need. Your plans will change.
4. **Build small, not large.** Write a few excerpts from the Book of Mekinekwa Wisdom, not the whole book.
5. **Stay organized.** Log everything into your notebook.

---

### Advanced Technique: Art and Artifacts

Everything your characters use, carry, wear, and live among. This is the largest category of advanced work.

#### ✅ Art and Artifacts Process Checklist

**Working process (in order):**

- [ ] Write descriptions of objects first (who uses it, how it's made, what it looks like, what it costs)
- [ ] Draw illustrations second (rough sketches on quadrille paper)
- [ ] Make physical samples last (only when you genuinely need to get inside the object)

**Object categories to consider:**

- [ ] Clothing (by role: singles, pairs, families; by occasion: daily, ceremonial, work, religious, governmental; by class; by gender; by age)
- [ ] Household items (furniture, bedding, cookware, lighting, storage)
- [ ] Tools (farming, crafting, navigation, warfare, medicine)
- [ ] Weapons and armor
- [ ] Vehicles (land, water, air, space)
- [ ] Ceremonial and religious objects
- [ ] Currency and trade goods
- [ ] Art objects (paintings, sculptures, tapestries, jewelry)
- [ ] Magical or scientific instruments

**For each artifact category:**

- [ ] Filed in: Home Life (A-b), Community (B-b), Religion (C-b), or Government (D-b)?
- [ ] Name (if your culture has a unique word for it)
- [ ] Brief description (what it looks like, how it's made)
- [ ] Who uses it and when
- [ ] Approximate cost or accessibility
- [ ] Page reference in your notebook

---

### Advanced Technique: The Cultural Library

Books, scrolls, oral histories, folk tales, epic poems, proverbs — the intellectual and artistic inheritance of your world.

#### ✅ Cultural Library Checklist

**For each invented work:**

- [ ] Category (Religion, Law, Folk Tales, How-To, Fantasy, History, Poetry, etc.)
- [ ] Type (Sacred text, commentary, biography, epic poem, forbidden tract, oral history)
- [ ] Author/Title
- [ ] Degree of authority within the culture (Highly respected? Heretical? Forbidden? Debated?)
- [ ] A one-line summary or a short quote
- [ ] Page reference in your notebook

**For each invented author:**

- [ ] Short biography (origin, formative experience, status in the culture)
- [ ] Works attributed to them
- [ ] Sample quotes or excerpts
- [ ] Whether this author might recur across multiple stories in the same world

---

### Advanced Technique: Music and Dance

#### ✅ Music and Dance Checklist

**Working process (in order):**

- [ ] List titles and composers/artists
- [ ] Write a few lines of lyrics or a summary
- [ ] Only compose the full piece if music plays a critical cultural role

**For each piece:**

- [ ] Category (Home Life, Community, Religion, Government)
- [ ] Composer/title
- [ ] Lyrics snippet (even one memorable line)
- [ ] Occasion for performance
- [ ] Who performs it? Who listens?
- [ ] Page reference in your notebook

**Types of music to consider:**

- [ ] Work songs and sea shanties
- [ ] Private lullabies and folk songs
- [ ] Religious music (services, funerals, weddings, fertility rites)
- [ ] Government/military music (march music, ceremonial fanfares)
- [ ] Community music (dances, festivals, coming-of-age gatherings)
- [ ] Secret or underground music (coded messages, resistance anthems)

---

### Advanced Technique: Housing and Architecture

#### ✅ Architecture Checklist

**Working process (in order):**

- [ ] List the types of buildings first (brief written descriptions)
- [ ] Draw floor plans and sketches only when listing isn't enough
- [ ] Build physical models only in extreme cases

**For each building type:**

- [ ] Category (Home Life, Community, Religion, Government)
- [ ] Builder/name (if notable)
- [ ] Description (materials, construction style, typical features)
- [ ] Who lives or works there
- [ ] Size and layout (even rough sketch)
- [ ] Unique or distinctive features worth describing in prose
- [ ] Page reference in your notebook

**Building types to consider:**

- [ ] Standard home for each class (poor, middle, wealthy)
- [ ] Extended family compounds
- [ ] Communal living arrangements
- [ ] Markets and commercial buildings
- [ ] Community gathering spaces (arenas, theaters, parks)
- [ ] Religious structures (temples, shrines, cathedrals, mosques)
- [ ] Government structures (courts, prisons, palaces, offices)

**For floor plans:**

- [ ] Where will characters hear sounds they shouldn't?
- [ ] Where are the secret passages?
- [ ] Which way does each window face? (Consistency across the story)
- [ ] Does the map of the town make organic sense, or was it grid-planned?

---

### Advanced Technique: Science and Magic

#### ✅ Science and Magic Checklist

**Establishing your system:**

- [ ] What are the underlying physical rules of how magic or advanced science works?
- [ ] What does it cost to use? (Energy? Time? Life? Sanity? Materials?)
- [ ] What are the hard limits?
- [ ] What happens when the rules are broken?
- [ ] Is it evenly distributed, or controlled by an elite?
- [ ] How is it learned or transmitted?

**For each specific application:**

- [ ] Category (Home Life, Community, Religion, Government)
- [ ] Type (science, magic, or both)
- [ ] Name and brief description
- [ ] Who uses it and in what context
- [ ] Its cost and limitations
- [ ] Page reference in your notebook

**Science/magic applications to consider:**

- [ ] Health and medicine
- [ ] Agriculture and food production
- [ ] Navigation (land, sea, space)
- [ ] Communication
- [ ] Warfare and protection
- [ ] Construction
- [ ] Religious ritual and communion with the divine
- [ ] Punishment and enforcement
- [ ] Commerce and trade

### Notebook Structure

Organize your culture notebook as follows:

```
INDEX (tabbed divider)
  ├── HOME LIFE INDEX (cover sheet)
  │     └── Cultural Background Sheet
  │     └── Cultural Artifacts Sheet (as needed)
  │     └── Cultural Library Sheet (as needed)
  │     └── Cultural Music Sheet (as needed)
  │     └── Buildings & Architecture Sheet (as needed)
  │     └── Science & Magic Sheet (as needed)
  ├── COMMUNITY INDEX (cover sheet)
  │     └── (same sheets as needed)
  ├── RELIGION INDEX (cover sheet)
  │     └── (same sheets as needed)
  └── GOVERNMENT INDEX (cover sheet)
        └── (same sheets as needed)

HOME LIFE — Section A (tabbed divider)
  └── Basic pages and advanced technique pages

COMMUNITY — Section B (tabbed divider)
  └── Basic pages and advanced technique pages

RELIGION — Section C (tabbed divider)
  └── Basic pages and advanced technique pages

GOVERNMENT — Section D (tabbed divider)
  └── Basic pages and advanced technique pages
```

---

### Page Numbering System

Every page you create should have:

- A **title** at the top
- A **category letter** in the top right corner: A (Home Life), B (Community), C (Religion), D (Government)
- An **information type letter**: a (basic info), b (art & artifacts), c (cultural library), d (music & dance), e (housing & architecture), f (science & magic), g (other)
- A **page number**

Example: Party clothing of the noble class → **A-b-1**

---

### ✅ Organization Master Checklist

- [ ] Notebook or binder set up with tabbed dividers
- [ ] Cover sheets and index pages in place for each section
- [ ] Every new page is titled, categorized, and numbered before filing
- [ ] Every new page is logged in the relevant index
- [ ] Physical samples are attached or referenced in the notebook
- [ ] A backup copy of all materials is stored somewhere safe

## Master Process Checklist

Use this as your top-level workflow guide each time you sit down to build culture.

### Before You Start

- [ ] Have you identified which section of the _story_ you're trying to enable? (Don't worldbuild in the abstract — build what the next scene needs)
- [ ] Have you skimmed the relevant question sections and marked a few questions that interest you?

### While You Work

- [ ] Are you answering questions that matter for _your_ story — not every question?
- [ ] After answering each question, are you deciding: **broad** (move to next question) or **deep** (use advanced techniques)?
- [ ] Are you following your own questions when they arise naturally?
- [ ] Are you staying organized — titling, numbering, and filing each page?

### When to Stop

- [ ] When you know enough to write the next scene or section, **stop and write**
- [ ] Return to culture-building when you get stuck, not before

### The Thinness Test (Run This Periodically)

- [ ] Does your world include people at every stage of the life cycle?
- [ ] Does it include people at every level of every trade and profession, even those the plot doesn't directly need?
- [ ] Does it include functional subcultures and countercultures alongside the main culture?
- [ ] Do people's lives feel like they have texture beyond the plot?
- [ ] Can you hear the sound of a world going about its business in the background?

## Quick Reference: The Seven Culture-Killers

Holly Lisle's most important warnings — the pitfalls that produce thin, unconvincing cultures:

1. **The Homogenous Age Group** — If everyone in your story is the same age, something is wrong. Life is a cycle. Include babies, elders, everyone between.

2. **The Cardboard Profession** — If every character exists to serve the plot, the world feels like a stage set. Reference people whose existence the _world_ needs, not just the story.

3. **The Assumed Culture** — If you're writing your own culture's present-day norms without making them explicit in the text, your story will be unreadable in twenty years.

4. **The Painted Backdrop** — If you can feel the illusion of a world — if nothing interesting exists beyond what's directly shown — you have thinness. More depth, more implication, more texture.

5. **The Rabble Counterculture** — If your countercultural group only defines itself by what it _opposes_, it isn't a culture. Give it positive goals, a complete philosophy, and something worth building.

6. **The Dysfunctional "Culture"** — Dysfunctional groups are not cultures. A dysfunctional culture is a dead culture. Know your functional baseline first.

7. **The Advanced Technique Trap** — Getting so deep into worldbuilding that you never write the book. Build only what the story needs, when it needs it.
