# The Complete Star System Creation Guide

## A Comprehensive Reference for Science Fiction Worldbuilders

---

> _A star system is not merely a sun with rocks orbiting it. It is a nested hierarchy of physical, chemical, biological, political, and economic conditions — each layer constrained by the ones beneath it, each layer making the ones above it possible._

---

## How to Use This Guide

Work **top-down**: stellar properties constrain planetary properties, which constrain atmospheric and hydrological properties, which constrain biology, which constrains civilization, which constrains economics and politics. Each section includes:

- **Concept reference** — the ideas and variables to consider
- **Checklists** — exhaustive decision lists to complete for each system
- **Quick tables** — reference data for fast decisions
- **Design notes** — narrative and tonal guidance for fiction writers

You do not need to complete every checklist for every system. A system that appears only once in passing needs only a handful of traits. A system that serves as a setting for extended fiction deserves the full treatment.

---

## Part I: The Star

The star is the engine of everything. Its mass, temperature, luminosity, age, and activity determine which orbits are habitable, how long life has had to evolve, and what kind of radiation the system's inhabitants contend with daily.

---

### 1.1 Stellar Classification

Stars are classified by **spectral type** (surface temperature and color) and **luminosity class** (size and evolutionary stage).

#### Spectral Types — The Morgan–Keenan System

| Type | Color        | Surface Temp (K) | Notes for Fiction                                                                                  |
| ---- | ------------ | ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| O    | Blue-violet  | 30,000–50,000+   | Extremely rare; short-lived (millions of years); hard radiation; unlikely life                     |
| B    | Blue-white   | 10,000–30,000    | Rare; intense UV; short stellar lifespan; exotic, dramatic settings                                |
| A    | White        | 7,500–10,000     | Uncommon; strong UV; lifespan ~1–2 billion years; marginal for complex life                        |
| F    | Yellow-white | 6,000–7,500      | Uncommon; somewhat UV-active; reasonable habitable zones                                           |
| G    | Yellow       | 5,200–6,000      | The "solar type"; familiar; Earth-like possibilities; classic setting                              |
| K    | Orange       | 3,700–5,200      | Common; long-lived (15–30 billion years); stable; excellent for life                               |
| M    | Red          | 2,400–3,700      | Most common (~70% of stars); very long-lived; small habitable zones; tidally locked planets likely |

#### Luminosity Classes

| Class   | Description           | Example    |
| ------- | --------------------- | ---------- |
| Ia / Ib | Supergiant            | Betelgeuse |
| II      | Bright giant          | —          |
| III     | Giant                 | Arcturus   |
| IV      | Subgiant              | Procyon    |
| V       | Main sequence (dwarf) | The Sun    |
| VI      | Subdwarf              | —          |
| VII / D | White dwarf           | Sirius B   |

**Combined notation:** G2V = solar-type main-sequence star. K5V = orange dwarf. M8III = red giant.

#### Special Stellar Variants

- **Variable stars** — brightness fluctuates; pulsating or rotating asymmetric spots; affects surface temperatures periodically
- **Flare stars** (UV Ceti type) — primarily M-dwarfs; irregular intense X-ray and UV flares; hazardous to unshielded life; common in fiction as dramatic hazards
- **Binary/multiple systems** — two or more stars in mutual orbit; adds orbital complexity; see Section 1.5
- **Subdwarfs** — low-metallicity old stars; smaller habitable zones; ancient systems
- **Neutron stars / pulsars** — remnants of supernovae; lethal radiation; no conventional habitable zones; exotic settings
- **Black holes** — extreme gravity wells; accretion disk radiation; time dilation opportunities; pure drama

### 1.2 The Habitable Zone

The **habitable zone** (HZ) — sometimes called the "Goldilocks zone" or **circumstellar habitable zone (CHZ)** — is the range of orbital distances at which liquid water can exist on a planetary surface given sufficient atmospheric pressure.

#### HZ Boundaries by Stellar Type

| Star Type | Inner HZ Edge (AU) | Outer HZ Edge (AU) | Notes                        |
| --------- | ------------------ | ------------------ | ---------------------------- |
| A5V       | ~1.5               | ~3.0               | High UV; short timeframe     |
| F5V       | ~1.1               | ~2.0               | Larger zone; workable        |
| G2V (Sun) | ~0.95              | ~1.70              | Reference standard           |
| K2V       | ~0.55              | ~1.05              | Smaller, but long-lived star |
| M5V       | ~0.15              | ~0.30              | Tidal locking likely within  |

> **Design note:** These are approximate. Atmospheric composition, albedo (reflectivity), greenhouse gases, ocean coverage, and magnetic field protection all push planets inside or outside the nominal HZ into or out of habitability. "Life" in fiction can be stranger than biology demands.

#### Extended Habitability Concepts

- **Subsurface ocean worlds** — ice-covered moons with tidal heating (Europa analogue); habitable far outside the CHZ
- **Tidally heated moons** — gas giant moons warmed by gravitational flexing
- **Rogue planets with thick atmospheres** — insulated by hydrogen; no star required
- **Geothermally active worlds** — internal radioactive decay sustains liquid water; independent of stellar flux

### 1.3 Stellar Age and System History

A star's age determines how long chemistry, geology, and biology have had to operate.

#### Implications of Age

| Age (Gyr) | State of System                                                          |
| --------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 0–0.5     | Still forming; proto-planetary disk; heavy bombardment; no advanced life |
| 0.5–1.5   | Late heavy bombardment ending; geology active; simple life possible      |
| 1.5–4.0   | Mature system; complex life possible after ~2 Gyr                        |
| 4.0–8.0   | Stable mature system; peak window for complex life and civilization      |
| 8.0–12.0  | Old system; low metallicity likely; long evolutionary history            |
| 12.0+     | Very ancient; potential for extremely old civilizations                  |

### 1.4 The Sky As Seen from a Planet

This is often neglected but deeply affects the _feel_ of a setting.

#### Factors Affecting Sky Appearance

- **Star color:** An M-dwarf makes the sky reddish-orange at noon; a blue-white A-star makes the sky a deep, intense blue
- **Sky color formula:** Sky color = scattered sunlight minus absorbed wavelengths + atmospheric composition
- **Atmospheric scattering:** Nitrogen/oxygen atmospheres scatter blue (Rayleigh scattering); high dust produces reds and oranges; dense CO₂ creates a yellowish sky
- **Star apparent size:** A star 0.8 AU away appears ~25% larger than the Sun does from Earth; at 0.2 AU (M-dwarf HZ), the star dominates the sky
- **Apparent color of the star from surface:** Not the same as spectral type; atmospheric filtering applies

### 1.5 Binary and Multiple Star Systems

Roughly half of solar-type stars are in multiple systems. Many M-dwarfs have close companions.

#### Orbital Configurations

- **S-type (circumprimary) orbit** — planet orbits one star; the companion is a distant "second sun"; stable if companion is far enough away
- **P-type (circumbinary) orbit** — planet orbits both stars together; must be well outside the stellar pair; stable if far enough out; dramatically different day/year cycle
- **L-type** — planet at a Lagrange point of a binary pair; rare and unstable over geological timescales

#### Stability Rules of Thumb

- S-type: stable if planet's semi-major axis < ~1/3 of stellar binary separation
- P-type: stable if planet's semi-major axis > ~2–4× the stellar binary separation
- Highly eccentric binary orbits destabilize planetary orbits more

## Part II: The Planetary System Architecture

Before designing individual planets, design the system's overall structure — the arrangement and types of bodies occupying orbital space.

---

### 2.1 System Architecture Overview

Planetary systems form from protoplanetary disks. Their final architecture is shaped by initial disk mass, stellar type, and dynamical processes including planetary migration.

#### The General Pattern

Modern planetary science suggests systems tend to follow broad patterns, though with enormous variation:

- **Inner rocky zone** (inside frost line): terrestrial planets, dense iron-silicate composition
- **Frost line transition**: asteroid belt analogues common here (unconsolidated material, or cleared by a nearby giant)
- **Outer gas/ice giant zone** (beyond frost line): gas giants, ice giants, and their moon systems
- **Outer debris disk**: comet/planetesimal reservoir (Kuiper Belt / Oort Cloud analogues)

#### Migration and Disruption

- Gas giants can migrate inward, disrupting inner rocky planet formation ("hot Jupiter" systems have few inner planets)
- Giant planet migrations can scatter asteroids and comets inward (increasing bombardment) or outward
- A "cold Jupiter" analogue in the outer system acts as a comet shield for inner planets — relevant for long-term habitability

#### System Typology

| Type                  | Description                                                       |
| --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Solar analogue        | Rocky inners, cold gas giant(s) out beyond frost line             |
| Hot Jupiter system    | Gas giant close to star; few stable inner orbits                  |
| Compact multi-planet  | Many rocky planets in tight orbits (common around K and M dwarfs) |
| Super-Earth dominated | One or more large rocky planets dominate the inner system         |
| Debris-rich           | Active asteroid belts, high bombardment rate; hazardous, dramatic |
| Sparse                | Few planets; wide orbital spacing                                 |

### 3.2 Orbital Mechanics

#### Key Orbital Parameters

- **Semi-major axis (a):** Average orbital radius; determines baseline stellar flux received
- **Eccentricity (e):** 0 = circular, up to 1 = parabolic escape; high eccentricity causes seasonal extremes beyond axial tilt effects
- **Orbital period (P):** The planet's "year"; scales with distance
- **Inclination (i):** Angle of orbit relative to stellar equatorial plane; affects eclipse geometry
- **Axial tilt (obliquity, ε):** Tilt of planet's rotational axis relative to its orbital plane; drives seasons
- **Orbital resonances:** Relationships between orbital periods of multiple planets (e.g., 2:1, 3:2); affect tidal forcing and orbital stability

#### Tidal Locking

For planets orbiting close to their star (especially M-dwarfs), **tidal locking** may synchronize the rotation period to the orbital period. One hemisphere perpetually faces the star (permanent day); the other faces away (permanent night). This profoundly affects:

- Atmospheric circulation (strong day-to-night wind currents)
- Temperature extremes (unless thick atmosphere redistributes heat)
- Habitability (terminator zone — the eternal twilight ring — may be the most hospitable region)
- Biology and culture (no conventional day/night cycle; the "sun" never moves)

#### Milankovitch Cycles

Over very long timescales, axial tilt and orbital eccentricity vary due to gravitational influences from other planets. These **Milankovitch cycles** drive ice ages and climate oscillations. Relevant for:

- Geological time periods and mass extinction events
- Civilizational context (current climate vs. geological history)
- Long-lived alien species who may remember prior ice ages

### 3.3 The Magnetic Field

The planetary magnetic field is critical and often overlooked. It is generated by a convecting liquid metal core (for terrestrial planets, liquid iron). It provides:

- **Atmospheric shielding** from stellar wind stripping
- **Surface radiation protection** from cosmic rays and stellar particle events
- **Navigation reference** for organisms and civilizations
- **Interaction with stellar wind** to produce phenomena like aurora

#### Magnetic Field Considerations

- **No field:** Atmosphere slowly stripped by stellar wind over billions of years; higher surface radiation; auroral phenomena absent; compasses useless
- **Weak field:** Marginal protection; extended polar regions with higher radiation; auroras at lower latitudes
- **Earth-strength field:** Good protection; polar auroras; surface life well-shielded
- **Strong field:** Intense radiation belts (Van Allen analogues); creates hazards for orbital operations and satellites; auroras widespread

#### Factors Affecting Field Strength

- Large liquid iron core: stronger field
- Fast rotation: stronger field (rotation drives convection)
- Young planet: more internal heat, more vigorous convection, stronger field
- Tidal heating from moons: sustains core convection in older planets

### 3.4 The Atmosphere

The atmosphere is the planet's weather machine, greenhouse, and life-support membrane.

#### Atmospheric Composition Categories

| Type          | Primary Gases         | Notes                                                   |
| ------------- | --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Primordial    | H₂, He                | Remnant from protoplanetary disk; typical of gas giants |
| Reducing      | H₂, CH₄, NH₃, H₂O     | Early terrestrial planets; no free O₂                   |
| CO₂-dominated | CO₂, N₂               | Venus-like (runaway greenhouse) or Mars-like (thin)     |
| N₂/O₂         | N₂ (~78%), O₂ (~21%)  | Earth-like; requires biological oxygen production       |
| N₂-dominated  | N₂ with trace gases   | Could be lifeless or life-bearing depending on O₂       |
| SO₂/volcanic  | SO₂, CO₂, H₂S         | Volcanically active worlds; toxic                       |
| Exotic        | Cl₂, HCN, noble gases | Highly speculative; possible on exotic worlds           |

#### Greenhouse Effect Scale

| CO₂ Concentration | Effect                                                           |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ~400 ppm (Earth)  | Mild modern warming                                              |
| 1%                | Significant warming; human tolerance limit for extended exposure |
| 5%                | Toxic to humans; strong greenhouse                               |
| 10–50%            | Very strong greenhouse; Venus-like trajectory                    |
| ~96% (Venus)      | Runaway greenhouse; ~465°C surface                               |

#### Pressure Categories

| Pressure (atm) | Description | Notes                                                            |
| -------------- | ----------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| <0.01          | Trace       | Essentially airless; no significant weather                      |
| 0.01–0.1       | Thin        | Insufficient for most terrestrial life; strong radiation         |
| 0.1–0.5        | Low         | Some weather; altitude sickness equivalent for humans            |
| 0.5–2.0        | Normal      | Earth-like range; life-supporting                                |
| 2–10           | Dense       | Pressure suits needed for humans; greater heat retention         |
| >10            | Crushing    | Extreme; possible deep-ocean analogues; flying creatures smaller |

### 3.5 Hydrography and Surface Water

Water is the most important liquid for life as we know it, but oceans of other substances are possible.

#### Ocean Types

| Liquid            | World Type                             | Temperature Range        |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Water (H₂O)       | Terrestrial / ocean worlds             | 0–100°C at 1 atm         |
| Methane (CH₄)     | Titan-analogue                         | –182 to –161°C           |
| Ethane (C₂H₆)     | Titan-analogue (lake)                  | –183 to –89°C            |
| Ammonia (NH₃)     | Cold water worlds with NH₃ anti-freeze | –78 to –33°C             |
| Sulfuric acid     | Venus-analogue (cloud ocean)           | Varies                   |
| Supercritical CO₂ | High pressure worlds                   | >31°C, >73 atm           |
| Subsurface H₂O    | Ice-covered moons                      | Stable under ice; varies |

#### Hydrographic Coverage

| Coverage % | Description                                       |
| ---------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| 0–5%       | Desert world; scattered endorheic basins          |
| 5–30%      | Arid; limited coastal regions; most land dry      |
| 30–60%     | Balanced; significant continents and ocean basins |
| 60–80%     | Ocean-dominated; scattered continents             |
| 80–95%     | Shallow seas; archipelago continents              |
| 95–100%    | Global ocean; no permanent surface land           |

#### Ice Coverage and Glaciation

- Polar ice caps form when axial tilt is low and poles receive little annual sunlight
- Snowball Earth conditions: runaway ice-albedo feedback can glaciate even equatorial regions
- High axial tilt (>54°) reduces polar-equatorial temperature differential; poles may be warmer than equatorial zones

### 3.6 Geology and Tectonics

The geological engine drives volcanism, mountain building, carbon cycling, and ultimately the long-term climate thermostat.

#### Tectonic Activity States

| State                  | Description                                           | Implications                                                      |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Active plate tectonics | Multiple moving plates; subduction; mountain building | Carbon cycle; varied terrain; volcanism; earthquakes              |
| Stagnant lid           | Single plate; heat escapes via volcanism only         | Less terrain diversity; less carbon cycling; episodic resurfacing |
| No tectonics           | Geologically dead; cooled interior                    | Smooth ancient terrain; no fresh mineral cycling                  |
| Super-heated           | Tidal or radiogenic heating extreme                   | Io-like; lava world; continuous resurfacing                       |

#### Geological Features That Matter for Fiction

- **Mountain ranges** — created by plate collisions; drainage divides; climate barriers; orographic precipitation
- **Rift valleys** — plates separating; volcanic activity; may fill with water to form seas
- **Shield regions** — ancient, stable cratons; oldest exposed rock; often flat
- **Volcanic provinces** — hot spots and island chains; mineral-rich soils; hazardous
- **Impact basins** — ancient meteor strikes; may now be seas; visible from orbit
- **Canyon systems** — river or tectonic origin; dramatic scale possible
- **Karst terrain** — soluble rock (limestone analogues); caves, sinkholes, towers

### 3.7 Moons

Moons profoundly affect their host planet. Earth's Moon stabilizes axial tilt, drives tides, and lit human nights. Some moons are more interesting than their planets.

#### Moon Size Categories

| Category  | Radius (km) | Examples              | Notes                                         |
| --------- | ----------- | --------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Irregular | <100        | Deimos, Phobos        | Captured; rocky; no significant gravity       |
| Small     | 100–500     | —                     | Minor tidal effect                            |
| Medium    | 500–1500    | Titania, Oberon       | Moderate tides; may have geological activity  |
| Large     | 1500–2500   | Europa, Titan         | Significant tides; potential habitability     |
| Major     | 2500+       | Luna, Ganymede, Titan | Strong tidal effects; major orbital influence |

#### Moon Habitability

Moons of gas giants in the outer system can be habitable via:

- **Tidal heating** (orbital eccentricity forced by resonance → internal flexing → heat)
- **Subsurface oceans** (ice shell over liquid water maintained by tidal heat)
- **Thick reducing atmospheres** (Titan-like)

| ~0.2 Gya   | Mammals                      |                                 |

### 4.2 Biosphere Architecture

Life does not simply exist; it organizes into ecosystems with energy flows, trophic levels, and ecological niches.

#### Energy Sources for Ecosystems

| Primary Energy Source              | Ecosystem Type | Notes                                                    |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Photosynthesis (stellar light)     | Phototrophic   | Most familiar; requires transparent atmosphere and light |
| Chemosynthesis (mineral oxidation) | Chemotrophic   | Deep-sea vents, subsurface; no light needed              |
| Radiotrophic                       | Radiotrophic   | Melanin-based; uses ionizing radiation; niche            |
| Thermosynthesis                    | Thermotrophic  | Temperature gradients; theoretical                       |
| Electric gradients                 | Electrotrophic | Some bacteria on Earth; speculative for macrolife        |

#### Photosynthesis Variation

The color of photosynthetic pigments depends on stellar spectrum:

- G-type star → green pigments optimal (as on Earth; reflects green)
- K-type (orange star) → red or black pigments may dominate (absorb orange/red efficiently)
- M-type (red star) → black or deep purple/red pigments; absorb near-infrared
- **Black plants** absorb all available wavelengths; likely on dim red dwarf stars

## Appendix B: Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

**1. The monoculture trap.** All cultures are internally diverse. Avoid designing a planet as "the desert world where everyone is stoic warriors." Continents, oceans, and climatic zones create different regional cultures even on a world with a unified government.

**2. Forgetting the day length.** A 14-hour day creates very different cultural rhythms than a 30-hour day or a tidally locked world with no day at all. Sleep cycles, meal patterns, and work schedules are all affected.

**3. Ignoring gravity.** 1.4g means a 70kg human feels like 98kg. Architecture is squatter. Athletics are harder. Cardiovascular disease is more common. Flight is less common in biology. Do not forget this.

**4. Implausible atmospheric stability.** An atmosphere rich in both oxygen and methane is not stable — they react. Think about what produces your gases and what would consume them; only geological or biological cycling maintains the balance.

**5. The single biome.** Even with extreme climates (desert world, ice world), there are transitional zones, polar caps, deep ocean environments, cave systems, and equatorial-polar gradients. Variation is the rule.

**6. Economics without imports.** Every world that trades has needs it cannot meet locally. The dependencies between worlds create stories. Always ask: what does this world need that it cannot provide?

**7. Perfect political stability.** There is no such thing. Even the most stable governments have factions, grievances, and generational conflicts. A world described as "unified and peaceful" invites the question: what is being suppressed?

**8. The forgotten moon.** If a world has a large moon, it shapes tides, culture, calendar systems, navigation, myth, and in many settings, colonization history. Do not add a moon and then never mention it.

**9. Ignoring stellar age.** A young star (1–2 Gyr) may simply not have had time for complex life to evolve. An old star (10+ Gyr) means the geological and biological history is immense. Age is story.

**10. Isolation without reason.** Every lost colony, hidden enclave, or pre-contact world has a reason it has not been found or contacted. The explanation of its isolation often contains the world's most interesting story.
