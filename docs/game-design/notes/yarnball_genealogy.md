# The Yarnball: Genealogy of a Design Concept

_Tracing Craig Perko's "yarnball" concept from its roots (2005) to its crystallization (2019)_

## What Is the Yarnball?

The yarnball is Perko's metaphor for game systems that are "big, soft, messy challenges" — interlocking meters and modular systems that generate emergent narrative through player choices. Instead of binary pass/fail survival, the yarnball creates a web of competing pressures where managing one thread tightens another, and the resulting tradeoffs _are_ the story.

> "My goal is to turn survival into a yarnball. A soft, complicated challenge that interlaces with a lot of other things. I also want to make sure that it unfolds into a narrative according to the choices the player makes."
> — Perko, "Space Survival Gameplay" (2019-06-18)

The key insight: **the designer doesn't write the narrative — the system does.** Meters are not optimization targets. They are narrative generators.

## Why This Matters for Our Game

The yarnball maps directly to QBN (quality-based narrative). Each meter is a quality, ship module choices gate which storylets fire, and faction-specific meter curves make each faction's narrative space feel mechanically different. This gives us emergent narrative from systems rather than scripted content — essential for a text MMORPG that needs to sustain long-term play without an army of content writers.

## The Genealogy

### Phase 1: The Atoms (2005–2006)

The earliest seeds: games should be built from modular, interconnected parts, and narrative should arise from system interactions rather than authored scripts.

**2005 — The "clockwork" component model**
Games as assemblies of modular, interconnected components. Each part affects the others. The designer builds the clockwork; the player turns the key.

**2006 — "Chu chu carnage" emergent plot theory**
NPCs as interconnected components whose cascading reactions _are_ the plot. Stories emerge from characters traveling through spaces, not from static placement. This is the first appearance of the idea that narrative is an emergent property of system interactions.

Source: `2006-04-03-chu-chu-carnage.md`

### Phase 2: Two Threads Develop Separately (2007–2013)

Two ideas that will eventually merge into the yarnball develop on independent tracks: **soft/attrition-based challenge** and **functional customization as narrative divergence**.

**2007 — Component plots and "long atoms"**
Perko introduces an atom-size taxonomy for narrative components (big/medium/small) and "long atoms" that cast temporal connections forward and backward. This is a proto-yarnball network topology — stories as interconnected webs, not linear chains.

Source: `2007-01-22-component-plots.md`

**2007 — Soft challenge via System Shock 2**
Analysis identifies attrition-based, soft challenge as superior to binary pass/fail. A challenge you _weather_ rather than _defeat_. This is half the yarnball — the "soft, complicated" part — but it's not yet connected to customization or narrative emergence.

**2008 — Roguelike critique: emergence isn't enough**
Emergent narrative alone is insufficient. Systems must be _designed_ for narrative richness — random interactions don't automatically produce good stories. "Stories must resonate with the game engine." This is the quality control principle that separates the yarnball from pure sandbox chaos.

**2009 — "The Outer Ring"**
Narrative identified as the outermost gameplay loop — the layer that gives long-term meaning to moment-to-moment mechanics. This is the structural _need_ that the yarnball will eventually fill: a system that generates ongoing narrative meaning from mechanical play.

Source: `2009-02-05-the-outer-ring.md`

**2013 — Relationship topology as narrative generator**
"War Stories and Power Rangers" proposes that abstract relationship networks ("tagging") are equivalent to spatial topology for generating emergent narrative. You don't need physical space to create emergent stories — you need interconnected relationships. This is the clearest precursor to the yarnball's web-of-connections model.

Source: `2013-03-06-war-stories-and-power-rangers.md`

**2013 — Functional ship customization**
Starship aesthetics post argues that ship customization should be functional, not cosmetic — your choices about what to install affect how the game plays. This is the other half of the yarnball (modular systems creating divergent experiences) but it's not yet connected to survival meters or narrative emergence.

Source: `2013-04-17-social-npcs.md`

### Phase 3: The Problem Gets Named (2015–2017)

Perko explicitly identifies the problems the yarnball will solve. All the ingredients are present but the unifying metaphor is missing.

**2015 — "Basebuilding With People": the proto-yarnball**
The single most important predecessor. Contains the line: _"it's something you weather, not something you defeat"_ — essentially the definition of a yarnball challenge four years before the term existed. Argues for rich mental simulation of NPCs who need social context, not just stat lists. People as the complicating factor that turns base-building from optimization into narrative.

Source: `2015-02-23-basebuilding-with-people.md`

**2015 — RimWorld critique: binary is boring**
Binary challenges are "boring." The creative/survival split is "too binary." Games need challenges that exist on a spectrum, where partial success and partial failure are both interesting. This is Perko working out what's wrong with existing survival games — the negative space that the yarnball will fill.

**2016 — "Building From the Inside"**
Environmental factors as implicit meters: noise, proximity to others, quality of infrastructure. These create interconnected tradeoffs where improving one thing worsens another. The meters aren't explicit yet, but the _topology_ of the yarnball is here — a web of competing environmental pressures.

Source: `2016-09-22-building-from-the-inside.md`

**2017 — Oxygen Not Included analysis**
Multiple approaches to one pressure create depth. ONI's interconnected resource systems (heat, oxygen, food, morale) are analyzed as a model for challenges that can't be "solved" — only managed through ongoing tradeoffs. This is the yarnball in everything but name.

### Phase 4: The Breakthrough (2018–2019)

The theoretical pieces click together. The Sims provides the missing model. The metaphor is born.

**2018 January — "The Sims vs Rimworld": the theoretical breakthrough**
Perko reverse-engineers why The Sims' meter system works where RimWorld's doesn't. The key insight: The Sims creates a **low-stress facility** where setbacks become _personal narratives_ ("my Sim's kitchen caught fire!") rather than _system failures_ ("I lost the game"). Meters work as narrative generators when the stakes are emotional/social rather than lethal. The player _cares_ about the meters because they care about the characters, not because failure means game over.

Source: `2018-01-09-the-sims-vs-rimworld.md`

**2018 January — "Designing Inhabitant-Centric Games"**
Applied design using The Sims' spatial/scheduling model. Four-element anatomy of emergent narrative: context, endings, management, and mix-ins. This is the blueprint for _how_ to build a yarnball — the structural components that make meter-driven narrative work.

Source: `2018-01-11-designing-inhabitant-centric-games.md`

**2018 September — "Character-Driven Game Design"**
Simple mechanics creating emergent narrative as the theoretical capstone. Context-driven progressions where NPCs propose action plans. The merger of narrative emergence with character systems.

Source: `2018-09-10-character-driven-game-design.md`

**2019 June 11 — "Boiling the Yarnball": the metaphor debuts**
The concept gets its name. Perko defines yarnball challenges as "big, soft, messy challenges" with entanglements. Introduces a four-phase model for how yarnball systems work: initial pressure → player response → cascading consequences → new pressure configuration. The yarnball is not a single mechanic — it's a _topology_ of interconnected, soft challenges.

Source: `2019-06-11-boiling-the-yarnball.md`

**2019 June 18 — "Space Survival Gameplay": the yarnball applied**
One week later, the yarnball is applied to space game design. Sims-like meters (hunger, filth, loneliness, claustrophobia, boredom) tick up per jump. Ship modules passively reduce meter growth. Player module choices create divergent narratives. Faction dossiers extend the metaphor to social systems. The full concept, fully realized.

Source: `2019-06-18-space-survival-gameplay.md`

## The Throughline

Two threads developed independently for over a decade:

1. **Soft challenge**: attrition-based, weathered-not-defeated, spectrum-not-binary (2007→2015→2017)
2. **Modular narrative divergence**: functional customization that changes how the game plays and what stories emerge (2005→2013→2016)

These merged in 2018 when The Sims provided the missing model: meters as narrative generators in a low-stress facility. The yarnball is the synthesis — interconnected soft challenges whose management through modular customization produces emergent narrative unique to each player.
