## Sunday, June 18, 2006 


### Links n' Stuff

I was recently asked for links n' stuff on hoity-toity game design. Here's some. If you have some, comment away.  
  
[MDA/8 Kinds of Fun](http://www.8kindsoffun.com/) (Poor website, IMO, but good ideas.)  
  
8 Kinds of Fun (also avaible in flavors of 7, 9, and 13, as I recall) is simply a way to get you to think about what kinds of experiences you'll be providing - helping you to either focus or diversify.  
  
MDA is a method of looking at how mechanics affect the end experience of the player - this mechanistic style of game design is pretty much all I go for, although I personally think MDA is a bit... "loose". It doesn't contain any real helpful things, just a kind of generic overview of ways you might want to think about stuff.  
  
[Poker player types](http://www.thehendonmob.com/index.php?id=157), which are actually very good analysis of game players in general, although they're missing several kinds of players because poker doesn't appeal to everyone.  
  
Maybe more useful, [4/25 types of players](http://www.kaila.pl/rpg/25classe.htm). Not the standard fare, but I never liked the standard fare. It's humor, but there's a lot of real data there. Which, really, is the reason it's funny.  
  
Of course, the standard fare is [Bartle's Four Player Types](http://www.mud.co.uk/richard/hcds.htm). I think this is an old essay, but I can't tell for sure. Anyhow, that's the most popular analysis of player types.  
  
When it comes to design paradigms, there aren't very many links available. MDA is one of the few (first link). There's a few others, like [the 400 project](http://www.theinspiracy.com/400_project.htm) and about a million ["patterns of game design" knockoffs](http://ludism.org/gamedesign/).  
  
Mostly, however, it's a tenuous gathering of posts from people who are thinking but not publishing. For example, [Skotos](http://www.skotos.net/articles/) , [Sirlin](http://www.sirlin.net/Features/feature_RuleDesign.htm) , and even [me](http://projectperko.blogspot.com/).  
  
The real problem is that the deeper theories come from people who have half a dozen theories. Me? I run with [PAC](http://projectperko.blogspot.com/2005/12/pac-pacpacpac.html) , [nested gameplay loops](http://projectperko.blogspot.com/2005/09/kaloki-halo-and-play-loops.html) , and [relative play vision](http://projectperko.blogspot.com/2005/11/shmups-and-relative-vision-redux.html) - all three of which provide for interesting, useful ways of looking at games. But it's not **a** theory. Most other theorists of this type are in the same boat.  
  
(Those links are old, because it's been a while since I've posted overviews. As time goes on, it's updates and new permutations...)  
  
There. Post your favorite links in the comments section.

Nicole Lazzaro's "Types of Fun" gives the most compact flavor, weighing in only at 4: http://www.xeodesign.com/whyweplaygames.html  
  
Chris Bateman's series on Roger Callois' types of play is a great reference:  
  
http://onlyagame.typepad.com/only\_a\_game/2006/05/roger\_caillois\_.html  
  
Clint Hocking's speech on "simulation boundary" is pretty solid: http://clicknothing.typepad.com/Design/hockingc\_GDC04\_Bishops.zip  
  
You're right about theories working best in packs. I tend to think in terms of "Magic Circles" ala Huizinga, with different "spins" applied based on Callois trio of dualities, and these different circles (which I think are similar to your play loop concept) work like a memetic molecule. Memetics actually strikes me as the most useful, though the lack of a formal foundation makes for a lower hit rate, but if there were it would integrate system dynamics with psychology and really provide some useful concepts.  
  
In the meantime I'll try my best to do what game designers have done for decades, apply concepts in an relatively ad-hoc manner while aiming for an underlying consistency.  
  
Also, it strikes me that rapid prototyping and a unified theory of game design are two approaches to the same quality, and they can do wonderful things in concert.
